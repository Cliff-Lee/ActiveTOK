Overview

Students compare visual, algebraic, and verbal proofs of the Pythagorean theorem and decide what makes each convincing.

TOK Fit

Category: Mathematics

Big idea: Mathematical proof is not just a result; it is a standard for why a result must be true.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

Student Prompt

Inspect Pythagorean Proof Gallery Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

Concrete Stimulus Packet

Stimulus

Pythagorean Proof Gallery Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Visual rearrangement proof of a2+b2=c2. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

Stimulus

Pythagorean Proof Gallery Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Algebraic proof card plus a verbal explanation card of the same theorem. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

Stimulus

Pythagorean Proof Gallery Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Algebraic proof card plus a verbal explanation card of the same theorem. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Mathematical proof is not just a result; it is a standard for why a result must be true.

Actual Lesson Questions and Key

# Question for students Teacher key / cue
1 After inspecting Pythagorean Proof Gallery Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim? Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2 Which exact detail from Pythagorean Proof Gallery Stimulus A did you use as evidence? Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3 What did you infer beyond the evidence? Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4 How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%? Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5 What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim? Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6 What makes mathematical proof different from empirical confirmation? Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how mathematical proof is not just a result; it is a standard for why a result must be true.

  • Strong response: separates observation from inference.
  • Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
  • Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
  • Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

Preparation

  • Three proof cards: visual rearrangement, algebraic derivation, and verbal explanation.
  • Proof evaluation sheet.
  • Notation card showing a2+b2=c2.

Setup Notes

  • Prepare three proof formats and do not identify one as best at the start.
  • Ask students to rank proof cards using different criteria: certainty, explanation, elegance, and accessibility.

Ready-to-Use Examples

  • Visual rearrangement proof of a2+b2=c2.
  • Algebraic proof card plus a verbal explanation card of the same theorem.

Run Resources

  • Proof evaluation grid: assumptions, conclusion, why it must be true, what it helps you understand.
  • Criteria cards: rigorous, explanatory, elegant, memorable, generalisable.

Before Class

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

Suggested Timing

Total time: 30-40 min

Stage Teacher move Facilitation note
1 Display three different proofs of a2+b2=c2. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
2 Groups annotate what each proof shows, assumes, and leaves unclear. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
3 Students rank proofs by convincingness, explanatory power, and elegance. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
4 Groups compare whether their rankings changed across criteria. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
5 Debrief why mathematical knowledge values proof rather than repeated measurement alone. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
Debrief Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective.

Sample Teacher Language

  • A proof does more than persuade; it should show why the result must hold.
  • Which proof gives certainty, and which proof gives understanding?

Facilitation Moves

  • Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
  • Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
  • Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
  • Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

Sample Student Output

  • The visual proof helped me understand area, but the algebraic proof felt easier to generalize.
  • Elegance made a proof more satisfying, but it was not the same as validity.

Likely Student Responses

  • Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
  • Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
  • Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
  • Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

Activity-Specific Adaptations

  • Support: focus on two proof cards.
  • Challenge: students write a fourth proof explanation for younger students.

Extension Tasks

  • Students compare a proof with many measured right triangles and explain why measurement is insufficient.
  • Connect mathematical elegance to aesthetic judgement in the arts.

Assessment Evidence

  • Students distinguish observation from interpretation.
  • Students explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
  • Students formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
  • Students use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

Debrief Questions

  • Knowledge question: What makes a proof convincing?
  • Can a visual proof explain better than an algebraic proof?
  • Is elegance a mathematical virtue or an aesthetic judgement?

Knowledge Questions

  • What makes mathematical proof different from empirical confirmation?
  • How do different representations support different kinds of understanding?
  • Does beauty play a role in mathematical knowledge?
