# Prompt Framing Lab Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students compare how different prompts can make the same AI-style task produce different answers and assumptions.

## TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and Technology

Big idea: A prompt is not just a request; it frames what kind of knowledge output is likely.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Prompt Framing Lab Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Prompt Framing Lab Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Neutral prompt: 'Explain whether homework improves learning.'. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Prompt Framing Lab Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Leading prompt: 'Explain why homework clearly improves learning and list supporting evidence.'. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Prompt Framing Lab Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Leading prompt: 'Explain why homework clearly improves learning and list supporting evidence.'. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — A prompt is not just a request; it frames what kind of knowledge output is likely.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Prompt Framing Lab Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Prompt Framing Lab Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Who is responsible for knowledge produced through a prompt?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how a prompt is not just a request; it frames what kind of knowledge output is likely.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Prompt cards with neutral, leading, vague, evidence-demanding, and role-based versions.
- AI-output comparison sheet or teacher-written mock outputs.

## Setup Notes

- Use teacher-written mock outputs if live AI access is not appropriate.
- Keep the task constant while changing only the prompt wording.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Neutral prompt: 'Explain whether homework improves learning.'
- Leading prompt: 'Explain why homework clearly improves learning and list supporting evidence.'

## Run Resources

- Prompt comparison grid: assumption, evidence requested, uncertainty shown, missing perspective.
- Prompt revision checklist: define task, request sources, ask for uncertainty, include counterarguments.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- The output begins before the machine answers; it begins with the frame we give it.
- What did your prompt make easy for the system to say?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The leading prompt produced a more confident answer even though the evidence was not stronger.
- Our revised prompt asked for uncertainty, so the answer became less fluent but more responsible.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: compare two prompts only.
- Challenge: add a source-checking phase where students verify one output claim.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students write a prompt policy for using AI in TOK research.
- Compare prompt framing with survey wording and loaded headlines.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: Who is responsible for knowledge produced through a prompt?
- Can a better question make a machine answer more reliable?
- When does a prompt smuggle in an assumption?

## Knowledge Questions

- Who is responsible for knowledge produced through a prompt?
- How does question design shape technological knowledge?
- Can prompts improve justification or merely improve fluency?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

Two prompts for the same task with visibly different outputs.
