# Counterfactual Forks Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students build careful counterfactuals to test causal claims without turning history into fantasy.

## TOK Fit

Category: History

Big idea: Counterfactual thinking can clarify causation when it is disciplined by evidence.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Counterfactual Forks Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Counterfactual Forks Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: School example: What if the viral incident had not happened, but earlier complaints still existed?. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Counterfactual Forks Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Historical prompt: What if one reform law had failed, while economic and social pressures remained?. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Counterfactual Forks Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Historical prompt: What if one reform law had failed, while economic and social pressures remained?. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Counterfactual thinking can clarify causation when it is disciplined by evidence.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Counterfactual Forks Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Counterfactual Forks Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Can historians know what did not happen?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how counterfactual thinking can clarify causation when it is disciplined by evidence.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Event cards with causes, constraints, and turning points.
- Counterfactual discipline checklist.

## Setup Notes

- Choose a causal story where one small change is plausible.
- Keep students close to evidence by requiring a constraint card for every counterfactual.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- School example: What if the viral incident had not happened, but earlier complaints still existed?
- Historical prompt: What if one reform law had failed, while economic and social pressures remained?

## Run Resources

- Counterfactual fork sheet: changed condition, fixed constraints, likely effects, uncertainty.
- Plausibility checklist: available actors, resources, beliefs, institutions, timing.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- A disciplined counterfactual changes one thing and respects the rest of the evidence.
- What does your fork reveal about causal importance?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- Removing the trigger delayed the policy but did not remove the long-term pressure.
- Our counterfactual showed the event was a catalyst rather than the only cause.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: provide the changed condition.
- Challenge: require likely, possible, and unlikely outcomes.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students add counterfactual notes to an existing cause web.
- Compare counterfactual reasoning in history with model testing in science.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: Can historians know what did not happen?
- When do counterfactuals clarify causation and when do they become fantasy?
- How do evidence and plausibility constrain historical imagination?

## Knowledge Questions

- Can historians know what did not happen?
- How can counterfactuals clarify causation?
- What constrains historical imagination?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A cause web with one turning point removed and likely consequences marked.
