# Log Scale Surprise Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Mathematical representations can make the same relationship look explosive, steady, or manageable.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Log Scale Surprise Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Log Scale Surprise Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Dataset: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64 over seven days. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Log Scale Surprise Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Question: does the graph show runaway growth, steady multiplicative growth, or both?. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Log Scale Surprise Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Notation card: $log10(1000) = 3$ shows how a multiplicative change becomes an equal step on a log scale. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Mathematical representations can make the same relationship look explosive, steady, or manageable.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Log Scale Surprise Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Log Scale Surprise Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How does mathematical representation shape understanding?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Materials or Stimulus

- A dataset that grows multiplicatively, such as doubling values.
- Linear and logarithmic graph templates.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Dataset: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64 over seven days.
- Question: does the graph show runaway growth, steady multiplicative growth, or both?
- Notation card: $log10(1000) = 3$ shows how a multiplicative change becomes an equal step on a log scale.

## Run Resources

- Linear and logarithmic graph templates for the same dataset.
- Graph purpose cards: communicate urgency, compare growth rates, inspect proportional change.

## Activity Steps

1. Give students a simple growing dataset.
2. Groups plot or inspect the same data on a linear graph and a logarithmic graph.
3. Students describe the story each graph seems to tell.
4. Groups identify which graph is better for different purposes.
5. Debrief how mathematical representation affects knowledge claims.

## Teacher Language

- Both graphs can be accurate, but they serve different questions.
- What does this scale make visually obvious, and what does it hide?
