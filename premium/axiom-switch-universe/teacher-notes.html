Back to Axiom Switch Universe

Overview

Students explore how changing assumptions changes mathematical truth.

TOK Fit

Category: Mathematics

Big idea: Axioms create mathematical worlds.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

Student Prompt

Inspect Axiom Switch Universe Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

Concrete Stimulus Packet

Stimulus

Axiom Switch Universe Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: A geometry rule change where parallel lines behave differently. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

Stimulus

Axiom Switch Universe Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: A made-up arithmetic system with a changed operation rule. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

Stimulus

Axiom Switch Universe Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: A reflection on whether certainty depends on starting assumptions. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Axioms create mathematical worlds.

Actual Lesson Questions and Key

# Question for students Teacher key / cue
1 After inspecting Axiom Switch Universe Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim? Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2 Which exact detail from Axiom Switch Universe Stimulus A did you use as evidence? Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3 What did you infer beyond the evidence? Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4 How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%? Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5 What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim? Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6 Are mathematical truths absolute? Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how axioms create mathematical worlds.

  • Strong response: separates observation from inference.
  • Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
  • Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
  • Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

Preparation

  • Three rule cards describing different parallel postulates.

Setup Notes

  • Prepare a starter stimulus using: A geometry rule change where parallel lines behave differently.
  • Print or project the student response grid before the activity begins.
  • Plan a private first-response moment before any group discussion.

Ready-to-Use Examples

  • A geometry rule change where parallel lines behave differently.
  • A made-up arithmetic system with a changed operation rule.
  • A reflection on whether certainty depends on starting assumptions.

Run Resources

  • Printable student worksheet with observation, inference, evidence, and confidence columns.
  • Teacher notes with setup checklist, sample facilitation language, misconceptions, and assessment look-fors.
  • Classroom run kit with prompt cards, example stimuli, and debrief moves.
  • PowerPoint deck with a student-facing sequence for running the activity.

Before Class

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

Suggested Timing

Total time: 35-45 min

Stage Teacher move Facilitation note
1 Give groups different geometry worlds: exactly one parallel line, no parallel lines, or many parallel lines through a point not on a line. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
2 Students draw what triangles, lines and maps might be like in their world. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
3 Each group states one theorem that may change. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
4 Discuss usefulness, consistency and truth. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
Debrief Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective.

Sample Teacher Language

  • Write your first judgement before we discuss it; the first answer is useful evidence.
  • Separate what you noticed from what you inferred.
  • What would make this knowledge claim more reliable?

Facilitation Moves

  • Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
  • Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
  • Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
  • Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

Sample Student Output

  • A student claim connected to Axiom Switch Universe: "My first judgement felt obvious, but my evidence was thinner than I realised."
  • A revised claim that names a method, perspective, or language choice that changed the result.
  • A knowledge question that moves beyond the activity example.

Likely Student Responses

  • Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
  • Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
  • Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
  • Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

Activity-Specific Adaptations

  • Short lesson: use only the first example, one response grid, and one debrief question.
  • Long lesson: add a second round where students revise the method and compare outcomes.
  • Low-tech option: run with printed cards, sticky notes, and a board tally.

Extension Tasks

  • Design a second version of the activity that tests the same knowledge issue in another AOK.
  • Find a real-world example where the same knowledge problem matters outside the classroom.
  • Write a 150-word TOK exhibition-style commentary using the activity as the object context.

Assessment Evidence

  • Students distinguish observation from interpretation.
  • Students explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
  • Students formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
  • Students use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

Debrief Questions

  • Knowledge question: Are mathematical truths absolute?
  • If different axiom systems are possible, what makes one useful?
  • Is consistency enough for mathematical knowledge?

Knowledge Questions

  • Are mathematical truths absolute?
  • If different axiom systems are possible, what makes one useful?
  • Is consistency enough for mathematical knowledge?
