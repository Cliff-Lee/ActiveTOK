Expertise Boundary Clinic Teacher Notes

Back to Expertise Boundary Clinic

Overview

Students decide when expert authority should be trusted, challenged, or limited across different knowledge claims.

TOK Fit

Category: Cross-AOK

Big idea: Expertise is powerful evidence within boundaries, but those boundaries must be identified.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

Student Prompt

Inspect Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

Concrete Stimulus Packet

Stimulus

Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Expert: climate scientist; claims about atmospheric data, energy policy, election strategy, and personal lifestyle choices. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

Stimulus

Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Expert: novelist; claims about narrative technique, historical fact, psychology, and moral value. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

Stimulus

Expertise Boundary Clinic Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Expert: novelist; claims about narrative technique, historical fact, psychology, and moral value. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Expertise is powerful evidence within boundaries, but those boundaries must be identified.

Actual Lesson Questions and Key

# Question for students Teacher key / cue
1 After inspecting Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim? Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2 Which exact detail from Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus A did you use as evidence? Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3 What did you infer beyond the evidence? Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4 How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%? Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5 What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim? Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6 When is expert authority a good reason to believe? Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how expertise is powerful evidence within boundaries, but those boundaries must be identified.

  • Strong response: separates observation from inference.
  • Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
  • Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
  • Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

Preparation

  • Expert profile cards and claim cards from different AOKs.
  • Boundary checklist: domain, evidence, uncertainty, conflict of interest, consensus.

Setup Notes

  • Create expert cards that include real strengths and clear limits.
  • Include claims that are inside, adjacent to, and outside the expert's domain.

Ready-to-Use Examples

  • Expert: climate scientist; claims about atmospheric data, energy policy, election strategy, and personal lifestyle choices.
  • Expert: novelist; claims about narrative technique, historical fact, psychology, and moral value.

Run Resources

  • Boundary checklist: domain, evidence, consensus, conflict of interest, uncertainty.
  • Claim sorting mat: inside expertise, adjacent, outside, needs more evidence.

Before Class

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

Suggested Timing

Total time: 30-40 min

Stage Teacher move Facilitation note
1 Give groups an expert profile and several claims the expert might make. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
2 Students decide which claims fall inside, near, or outside the expert's domain. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
3 Groups identify what extra evidence would be needed for each claim. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
4 Compare cases where expertise should carry more or less weight. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
5 Debrief responsible trust in knowledge communities. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
Debrief Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective.

Sample Teacher Language

  • Respecting expertise includes knowing its boundaries.
  • What would make this expert's claim stronger or weaker evidence?

Facilitation Moves

  • Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
  • Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
  • Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
  • Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

Sample Student Output

  • The expert had strong authority on data interpretation but less on policy trade-offs.
  • We trusted the expert more when they stated uncertainty and evidence limits.

Likely Student Responses

  • Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
  • Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
  • Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
  • Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

Activity-Specific Adaptations

  • Support: use one expert and four claim cards.
  • Challenge: include a conflict of interest or expert disagreement.

Extension Tasks

  • Students evaluate an expert source they might use for a TOK exhibition commentary.
  • Compare expert authority in medicine, art criticism, law, and mathematics.

Assessment Evidence

  • Students distinguish observation from interpretation.
  • Students explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
  • Students formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
  • Students use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

Debrief Questions

  • Knowledge question: When is expert authority a good reason to believe?
  • How can knowers respect expertise without surrendering judgement?
  • What counts as overreach beyond a domain of knowledge?

Knowledge Questions

  • When is expert authority a good reason to believe?
  • How should knowers identify the limits of expertise?
  • Can trust and critical thinking work together?
