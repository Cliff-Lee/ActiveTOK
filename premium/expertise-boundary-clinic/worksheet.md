# Expertise Boundary Clinic Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Expertise is powerful evidence within boundaries, but those boundaries must be identified.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Expert: climate scientist; claims about atmospheric data, energy policy, election strategy, and personal lifestyle choices. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Expert: novelist; claims about narrative technique, historical fact, psychology, and moral value. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Expertise Boundary Clinic Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Expert: novelist; claims about narrative technique, historical fact, psychology, and moral value. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Expertise is powerful evidence within boundaries, but those boundaries must be identified.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Expertise Boundary Clinic Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. When is expert authority a good reason to believe?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Materials or Stimulus

- Expert profile cards and claim cards from different AOKs.
- Boundary checklist: domain, evidence, uncertainty, conflict of interest, consensus.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Expert: climate scientist; claims about atmospheric data, energy policy, election strategy, and personal lifestyle choices.
- Expert: novelist; claims about narrative technique, historical fact, psychology, and moral value.

## Run Resources

- Boundary checklist: domain, evidence, consensus, conflict of interest, uncertainty.
- Claim sorting mat: inside expertise, adjacent, outside, needs more evidence.

## Activity Steps

1. Give groups an expert profile and several claims the expert might make.
2. Students decide which claims fall inside, near, or outside the expert's domain.
3. Groups identify what extra evidence would be needed for each claim.
4. Compare cases where expertise should carry more or less weight.
5. Debrief responsible trust in knowledge communities.

## Teacher Language

- Respecting expertise includes knowing its boundaries.
- What would make this expert's claim stronger or weaker evidence?
