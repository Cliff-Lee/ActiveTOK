# Anomaly Rescue Mission Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students receive a dataset with one anomalous result and decide whether to reject, explain, repeat, or revise the model.

## TOK Fit

Category: Natural Sciences

Big idea: Anomalies are not automatically errors; they are tests of method, theory, and judgement.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Anomaly Rescue Mission Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Anomaly Rescue Mission Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Plant growth mostly increases with light exposure, except one low-light plant grows unusually tall. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Anomaly Rescue Mission Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Reaction time improves with practice, except one trial suddenly becomes much slower. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Anomaly Rescue Mission Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Reaction time improves with practice, except one trial suddenly becomes much slower. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Anomalies are not automatically errors; they are tests of method, theory, and judgement.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Anomaly Rescue Mission Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Anomaly Rescue Mission Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. When should anomalous evidence change a theory?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how anomalies are not automatically errors; they are tests of method, theory, and judgement.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Small fictional dataset with one outlier or anomalous result.
- Decision cards: measurement error, uncontrolled variable, new phenomenon, repeat trial, revise model.

## Setup Notes

- Use a dataset simple enough for students to understand quickly but ambiguous enough to support different responses.
- Make the anomaly plausible as either error or discovery.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Plant growth mostly increases with light exposure, except one low-light plant grows unusually tall.
- Reaction time improves with practice, except one trial suddenly becomes much slower.

## Run Resources

- Anomaly decision board: discard, repeat, investigate, revise model.
- Evidence-needed cards: check instrument, repeat measurement, inspect conditions, compare theory.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 30-40 min

| Stage | Teacher move | Facilitation note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Give groups a simple model and a dataset that mostly fits it. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 2 | Students identify the anomalous result and list possible explanations. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 3 | Groups choose a response: discard, repeat, investigate, or revise the model. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 4 | Groups defend what evidence would justify their response. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 5 | Debrief how scientists should treat results that do not fit expectation. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- An anomaly is a question mark, not automatically a mistake or a breakthrough.
- What evidence would justify your next move?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- We repeated the measurement before revising the model because one point was not enough.
- The anomaly mattered because it appeared under a condition the model had ignored.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: give three possible explanations to choose from.
- Challenge: include two anomalies with different likely causes.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students write a cautious lab-note response to an unexpected result.
- Compare famous scientific anomalies with everyday data cleaning.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: When should anomalous evidence change a theory?
- How can expectations help and hinder scientific judgement?
- What is the difference between ignoring an anomaly and responsibly investigating it?

## Knowledge Questions

- When should anomalous evidence change a theory?
- How do expectations shape interpretation of data?
- What makes an outlier scientifically important?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A dataset graph with one anomalous point and competing explanation labels.
