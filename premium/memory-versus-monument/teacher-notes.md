# Memory versus Monument Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students compare personal memory, public memorials, and archival evidence as different ways of knowing the past.

## TOK Fit

Category: History

Big idea: Public memory and historical knowledge overlap, but they are not the same practice.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Memory versus Monument Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Memory versus Monument Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Fictional memorial: a plaque celebrating a former headteacher beside a diary extract from a student and a board meeting note. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Memory versus Monument Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Comparison prompt: What does each source ask the public to remember?. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Memory versus Monument Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Comparison prompt: What does each source ask the public to remember?. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Public memory and historical knowledge overlap, but they are not the same practice.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Memory versus Monument Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Memory versus Monument Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How is public memory different from historical knowledge?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how public memory and historical knowledge overlap, but they are not the same practice.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Setup Notes

- Use a local or familiar memorial only if the class context is suitable; otherwise use a fictional school memorial.
- Prepare three sources with clearly different purposes.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Fictional memorial: a plaque celebrating a former headteacher beside a diary extract from a student and a board meeting note.
- Comparison prompt: What does each source ask the public to remember?

## Run Resources

- Source comparison chart: purpose, evidence, emotion, omission, reliability.
- Memory/history Venn diagram.

## Sample Teacher Language

- A memorial is not a textbook page; it is a public act of selection.
- What kind of truth is the memorial trying to preserve?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The plaque communicated gratitude, while the archive showed conflict and compromise.
- The memorial was evidence of values as much as evidence of the event.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use a fictional school example.
- Challenge: include two public audiences with competing needs.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students design a revised plaque with a short note about what it includes and excludes.
- Connect to debates about museums, statues, and national memory.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How is public memory different from historical knowledge?
- Can a monument be evidence, interpretation, and argument at once?
- Whose responsibility is it to revise public memory?

## Knowledge Questions

- How is public memory different from historical knowledge?
- What can monuments help us know and what can they hide?
- How do emotion and evidence interact in history?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A photograph of a monument annotated for evidence, values, and omissions.
