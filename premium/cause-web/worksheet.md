# Cause Web Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Causation is layered and contestable.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Cause Web Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Cause Web Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: A web of causes for a school rule change, historical event, or social movement. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Cause Web Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Cards sorted into trigger, background condition, actor, and consequence. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Cause Web Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: A debate over whether one cause can be 'the' cause. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Causation is layered and contestable.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Cause Web Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Cause Web Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How do historians decide which causes matter most?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Activity Steps

1. Students create a web of causes.
2. They label causes as long-term, short-term, structural, individual, accidental or ideological.
3. They choose the three most important causes and justify their ranking.
4. Groups compare rankings.

## Teacher Language

- Write your first judgement before we discuss it; the first answer is useful evidence.
- Separate what you noticed from what you inferred.
- What would make this knowledge claim more reliable?
