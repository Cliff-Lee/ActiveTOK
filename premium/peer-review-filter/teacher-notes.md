# Peer Review Filter Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students act as reviewers for a fictional study and decide whether flaws require revision, rejection, or cautious acceptance.

## TOK Fit

Category: Natural Sciences

Big idea: Scientific knowledge is filtered by community criticism, not just produced by individual discovery.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Peer Review Filter Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Peer Review Filter Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Study claim: a lavender scent improves memory scores by 18% in one class of 18 students. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Peer Review Filter Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Flaws: no random assignment, small sample, unclear scoring, but transparent method and plausible follow-up. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Peer Review Filter Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Flaws: no random assignment, small sample, unclear scoring, but transparent method and plausible follow-up. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Scientific knowledge is filtered by community criticism, not just produced by individual discovery.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Peer Review Filter Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Peer Review Filter Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How does criticism strengthen knowledge?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how scientific knowledge is filtered by community criticism, not just produced by individual discovery.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- One-page fictional study summary with method, results, and conclusion.
- Reviewer checklist and decision cards.

## Setup Notes

- Write the fictional study so it has strengths as well as flaws.
- Give reviewers different priorities to simulate real disagreement.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Study claim: a lavender scent improves memory scores by 18% in one class of 18 students.
- Flaws: no random assignment, small sample, unclear scoring, but transparent method and plausible follow-up.

## Run Resources

- Reviewer checklist: sample, control, measurement, claim size, alternative explanation, ethical issue.
- Decision card: accept, minor revision, major revision, reject, or invite replication.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

Total time: 30-40 min

## Sample Teacher Language

- Peer review is not a truth machine; it is organized criticism.
- Which flaw changes the conclusion, and which flaw can be repaired?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- We recommended major revision because the claim was larger than the method justified.
- The study was interesting but not yet reliable enough for a strong headline.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: provide three marked flaws.
- Challenge: include conflicting reviewer recommendations and require an editor decision.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students rewrite the study conclusion to match the evidence more responsibly.
- Compare peer review with fact-checking, editing, or legal cross-examination.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How does criticism strengthen knowledge?
- Does peer review guarantee truth or reduce risk?
- What should a scientific community do with promising but flawed evidence?

## Knowledge Questions

- How does criticism strengthen knowledge?
- What role do communities play in scientific reliability?
- When is evidence strong enough to publish?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A marked-up fictional study with reviewer comments in the margins.
