Data Dashboard Mirage Teacher Notes

Back to Data Dashboard Mirage

Overview

Students compare two dashboards built from the same data and evaluate how interface design shapes conclusions.

TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and Technology

Big idea: Dashboards do not simply show data; they prioritize, compress, and narrate it.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

Student Prompt

Inspect Data Dashboard Mirage Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

Concrete Stimulus Packet

Stimulus

Data Dashboard Mirage Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Dashboard A highlights falling late arrivals in green; Dashboard B highlights rising absences in amber using the same school-week dataset. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

Stimulus

Data Dashboard Mirage Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Dashboard A ranks classes by average score; Dashboard B ranks them by improvement. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

Stimulus

Data Dashboard Mirage Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Dashboard A ranks classes by average score; Dashboard B ranks them by improvement. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Dashboards do not simply show data; they prioritize, compress, and narrate it.

Actual Lesson Questions and Key

# Question for students Teacher key / cue
1 After inspecting Data Dashboard Mirage Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim? Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2 Which exact detail from Data Dashboard Mirage Stimulus A did you use as evidence? Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3 What did you infer beyond the evidence? Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4 How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%? Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5 What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim? Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6 When does data visualization become argument? Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how dashboards do not simply show data; they prioritize, compress, and narrate it.

  • Strong response: separates observation from inference.
  • Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
  • Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
  • Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

Preparation

  • Two mock dashboards using the same fictional dataset.
  • Dashboard audit checklist.

Setup Notes

  • Use the same fictional dataset in both dashboard versions.
  • Change only visual design choices such as color, order, scale, and highlighted metric.

Ready-to-Use Examples

  • Dashboard A highlights falling late arrivals in green; Dashboard B highlights rising absences in amber using the same school-week dataset.
  • Dashboard A ranks classes by average score; Dashboard B ranks them by improvement.

Run Resources

  • Dashboard audit checklist: title, color, scale, missing context, default ordering, highlighted number.
  • Redesign template for a more transparent dashboard.

Before Class

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

Suggested Timing

Total time: 30-40 min

Stage Teacher move Facilitation note
1 Show students two dashboards with the same underlying data but different layouts. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
2 Groups identify the story each dashboard seems to tell. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
3 Students trace which design choices create that impression. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
4 Groups redesign one dashboard for greater transparency. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
5 Debrief how interfaces shape what users think they know. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
Debrief Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective.

Sample Teacher Language

  • A dashboard answers before we know what question it was built to answer.
  • Which design choice did the most interpretive work?

Facilitation Moves

  • Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
  • Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
  • Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
  • Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

Sample Student Output

  • The green metric made the school look successful even though absences were rising.
  • Both dashboards were accurate, but each made a different issue feel urgent.

Likely Student Responses

  • Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
  • Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
  • Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
  • Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

Activity-Specific Adaptations

  • Support: compare two dashboards with one obvious design difference.
  • Challenge: ask students to write a dashboard ethics checklist.

Extension Tasks

  • Students redesign a school dashboard for students, parents, and teachers separately.
  • Connect dashboard design to public health, climate, or finance communication.

Assessment Evidence

  • Students distinguish observation from interpretation.
  • Students explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
  • Students formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
  • Students use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

Debrief Questions

  • Knowledge question: When does data visualization become argument?
  • Can a dashboard be accurate and still misleading?
  • What should designers owe users who rely on visualized data?

Knowledge Questions

  • When does data visualization become argument?
  • How do interfaces shape knowledge?
  • What makes a technological representation responsible?
