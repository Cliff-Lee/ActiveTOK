# Source Amnesia Trace Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students hear claims from different sources, then later test whether they remember the claim, the source, or both.

## TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and the Knower

Big idea: Remembering a claim is not the same as remembering why it deserved trust.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Source Amnesia Trace Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Source Amnesia Trace Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Claim: 'Spacing revision improves long-term memory' paired first with 'cognitive scientist', then later shown without source. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Source Amnesia Trace Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Claim: 'Blue light glasses double focus' paired first with 'online advert', then later shown without source. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Source Amnesia Trace Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Claim: 'Blue light glasses double focus' paired first with 'online advert', then later shown without source. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Remembering a claim is not the same as remembering why it deserved trust.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Source Amnesia Trace Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Source Amnesia Trace Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. What happens to justification when we forget the source of a belief?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how remembering a claim is not the same as remembering why it deserved trust.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Claim cards paired with source labels such as expert, friend, advert, textbook, anonymous post.
- Delayed source-trace answer sheet.

## Setup Notes

- Pair each claim with a visibly different source label, then remove the labels for the recall phase.
- Use a short distractor task between exposure and recall so source memory has time to fade.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Claim: 'Spacing revision improves long-term memory' paired first with 'cognitive scientist', then later shown without source.
- Claim: 'Blue light glasses double focus' paired first with 'online advert', then later shown without source.

## Run Resources

- Source-trace answer sheet with columns: remembered claim, remembered source, confidence, revised trust.
- Source label cards: expert, textbook, friend, advert, anonymous post, personal experience.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 20-30 min

| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Show students a short set of claims, each paired with a different source type. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 2 | After a distractor task, give students the claims again without sources. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 3 | Students mark whether they remember the claim and then identify its original source. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- A claim can feel familiar even when its original justification has disappeared.
- What should happen to trust when source memory is weak?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- I remembered the claim but not that it came from an advert, so I trusted it too quickly.
- The source changed whether the same sentence counted as good evidence.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use six claims and three source types.
- Challenge: include two claims that are true but from weak sources and false but from confident sources.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students audit one belief they hold and trace where it probably came from.
- Connect source amnesia to citation, rumours, and social media reposting.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: What happens to justification when we forget the source of a belief?
- Can a claim become more or less credible in memory?
- How should knowers track where their beliefs came from?

## Knowledge Questions

- What happens to justification when we forget the source of a belief?
- Is remembering that something is true enough for knowledge?
- How does source memory affect responsible knowing?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A claim card with its original source label removed and later restored.
