# Source Silence Investigation Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Historical knowledge depends not only on surviving sources, but also on recognizing what did not survive.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Source Silence Investigation Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Source Silence Investigation Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Packet: official school notice, newspaper paragraph, teacher diary, but no student voices. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Source Silence Investigation Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Packet: merchant account, travel diary, law code, but no record from workers affected by the policy. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Source Silence Investigation Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Packet: merchant account, travel diary, law code, but no record from workers affected by the policy. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Historical knowledge depends not only on surviving sources, but also on recognizing what did not survive.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Source Silence Investigation Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Source Silence Investigation Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How can we know responsibly when evidence is missing?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Materials or Stimulus

- Fictional or real source packet with several perspectives present and at least one missing.
- Silence audit sheet.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Packet: official school notice, newspaper paragraph, teacher diary, but no student voices.
- Packet: merchant account, travel diary, law code, but no record from workers affected by the policy.

## Run Resources

- Silence audit sheet: present voices, missing voices, possible reasons, impact on conclusion.
- Uncertainty language bank: possibly, likely, cannot establish, source packet suggests.

## Activity Steps

1. Give groups a short source packet about one event.
2. Students list whose voices, records, or perspectives are present.
3. Groups infer which voices may be missing and why.
4. Students revise one conclusion to include uncertainty from source silence.
5. Debrief how historians responsibly reason from incomplete archives.

## Teacher Language

- Naming a silence is not the same as filling it with imagination.
- How does the missing voice change the strength of your conclusion?
