# Source Silence Investigation Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students investigate a historical source packet by asking whose voices are missing and how silence shapes conclusions.

## TOK Fit

Category: History

Big idea: Historical knowledge depends not only on surviving sources, but also on recognizing what did not survive.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Source Silence Investigation Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Source Silence Investigation Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Packet: official school notice, newspaper paragraph, teacher diary, but no student voices. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Source Silence Investigation Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Packet: merchant account, travel diary, law code, but no record from workers affected by the policy. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Source Silence Investigation Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Packet: merchant account, travel diary, law code, but no record from workers affected by the policy. Mark what the source shows, what it omits, whose perspective appears, and what claim it can responsibly support.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Historical knowledge depends not only on surviving sources, but also on recognizing what did not survive.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Source Silence Investigation Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Source Silence Investigation Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How can we know responsibly when evidence is missing?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how historical knowledge depends not only on surviving sources, but also on recognizing what did not survive.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Fictional or real source packet with several perspectives present and at least one missing.
- Silence audit sheet.

## Setup Notes

- Prepare a packet where the missing perspective is inferable but not recoverable.
- Make students mark uncertainty rather than inventing missing testimony.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Packet: official school notice, newspaper paragraph, teacher diary, but no student voices.
- Packet: merchant account, travel diary, law code, but no record from workers affected by the policy.

## Run Resources

- Silence audit sheet: present voices, missing voices, possible reasons, impact on conclusion.
- Uncertainty language bank: possibly, likely, cannot establish, source packet suggests.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- Naming a silence is not the same as filling it with imagination.
- How does the missing voice change the strength of your conclusion?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The archive lets us know the policy existed but not how students experienced it.
- We revised our claim from 'students accepted it' to 'the available sources do not show student opposition'.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: provide a checklist of possible missing voices.
- Challenge: ask students to distinguish accidental absence from deliberate exclusion.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students add a missing-source request: what source would most improve the account?
- Connect source silence to museums, family histories, and national archives.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How can we know responsibly when evidence is missing?
- Is absence of evidence evidence of absence?
- How do preservation and power shape historical knowledge?

## Knowledge Questions

- How can we know responsibly when evidence is missing?
- How do archives shape historical knowledge?
- What does source silence reveal about power?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A source packet annotated with present voices and missing voices.
