# Silence and Implication Lab Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Meaning often depends on implication; silence can communicate without explicitly claiming.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Silence and Implication Lab Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Silence and Implication Lab Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Dialogue: 'Did you like my presentation?' 'Well, your slides were very colorful.'. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Silence and Implication Lab Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Dialogue: 'Are you coming to the meeting?' 'I have a lot of homework tonight.'. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Silence and Implication Lab Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Dialogue: 'Are you coming to the meeting?' 'I have a lot of homework tonight.'. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Meaning often depends on implication; silence can communicate without explicitly claiming.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Silence and Implication Lab Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Silence and Implication Lab Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Can silence be evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Materials or Stimulus

- Implication analysis sheet.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Dialogue: 'Did you like my presentation?' 'Well, your slides were very colorful.'
- Dialogue: 'Are you coming to the meeting?' 'I have a lot of homework tonight.'

## Run Resources

- Explicit/implied meaning chart.
- Context cards: close friend, teacher feedback, formal email, sarcastic tone, nervous speaker.

## Activity Steps

1. Give pairs a short dialogue with one ambiguous silence or indirect reply.
2. Students list what was explicitly said and what was implied.
3. Groups add context cards that change the implication.
4. Students decide which interpretation is most justified.
5. Debrief how implication functions in everyday and institutional knowledge.

## Teacher Language

- What was said, what was implied, and what did you add?
- Which context clues justify your reading?
