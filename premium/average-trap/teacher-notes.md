# Average Trap Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students compare mean, median, and mode for skewed datasets and evaluate which average supports the most responsible claim.

## TOK Fit

Category: Mathematics

Big idea: An average is a choice of representation, not a neutral summary of reality.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Average Trap Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Average Trap Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Revision hours: 1, 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 18. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Average Trap Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Screen time claim: mean looks high, median looks moderate, mode shows the most common routine. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Average Trap Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Screen time claim: mean looks high, median looks moderate, mode shows the most common routine. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — An average is a choice of representation, not a neutral summary of reality.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Average Trap Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Average Trap Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How does choosing a summary statistic shape knowledge?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how an average is a choice of representation, not a neutral summary of reality.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Small datasets for incomes, screen time, revision hours, or class scores.
- Average card showing $mean = Σx / n$, median, and mode.
- Claim cards using different averages.

## Setup Notes

- Choose skewed datasets where one large value pulls the mean away from most cases.
- Ask students which number they would put in a headline before calculating all averages.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Revision hours: 1, 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 18.
- Screen time claim: mean looks high, median looks moderate, mode shows the most common routine.

## Run Resources

- Average comparison sheet: mean, median, mode, purpose, audience, and possible misuse.
- Headline cards using each average to support a different conclusion.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- All three averages can be correct; the TOK question is which one responsibly represents the situation.
- What does this number make visible, and what does it hide?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The mean was mathematically correct but made most students look unlike the typical case.
- The median was better for a typical value, but the mean was useful for total resource planning.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: give pre-calculated averages.
- Challenge: ask students to design a dataset where the three averages tell three different stories.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students find a public average and ask whether mean, median, or mode would be more responsible.
- Compare average choice with graph scale choice.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: When does a mathematical summary become misleading?
- Who decides which average is most relevant?
- Can a number be accurate but unfair?

## Knowledge Questions

- How does choosing a summary statistic shape knowledge?
- What makes a mathematical representation fair?
- When is simplification necessary, and when is it distortion?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A skewed dataset with mean, median, and mode labelled as competing summaries.
