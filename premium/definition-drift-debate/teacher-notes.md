# Definition Drift Debate Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students trace how a key term changes across contexts and debate whether changing definitions change knowledge.

## TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and Language

Big idea: Definitions stabilize inquiry, but they can also shift with purpose, power, and context.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Definition Drift Debate Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Definition Drift Debate Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Term: intelligence; definitions from test performance, adaptive behavior, creativity, social understanding, and problem-solving. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Definition Drift Debate Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Term: art; definitions from intention, institution, skill, audience response, and originality. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Definition Drift Debate Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Term: art; definitions from intention, institution, skill, audience response, and originality. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Definitions stabilize inquiry, but they can also shift with purpose, power, and context.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Definition Drift Debate Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Definition Drift Debate Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Do definitions describe reality or help construct it?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how definitions stabilize inquiry, but they can also shift with purpose, power, and context.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Definition cards for one term across dictionaries, institutions, everyday use, and specialist fields.
- Debate role cards.

## Setup Notes

- Pick one term with genuine but manageable ambiguity.
- Use a specific purpose for the debate, such as school policy, research, classification, or exhibition commentary.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Term: intelligence; definitions from test performance, adaptive behavior, creativity, social understanding, and problem-solving.
- Term: art; definitions from intention, institution, skill, audience response, and originality.

## Run Resources

- Definition comparison table: includes, excludes, evidence needed, who benefits.
- Borderline case cards that test each definition.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- A definition is doing work. What work is this definition doing?
- Which cases become easier or harder to know under this definition?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The test-score definition made measurement easy but excluded creativity.
- The definition changed what evidence counted as relevant.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use two definitions and three cases.
- Challenge: ask students to identify power effects in definition choices.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students write a definition with a clear purpose and a named limitation.
- Compare definitions across two languages or institutions.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: Do definitions describe reality or help construct it?
- Who should have authority to define contested terms?
- When does changing a definition improve knowledge and when does it manipulate it?

## Knowledge Questions

- Do definitions describe reality or help construct it?
- How do definitions shape evidence and disagreement?
- Who has authority over shared meanings?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A set of competing definition cards for one contested term.
