# Curator Choice Gallery Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Curators do not only display knowledge in the arts; they help organize and produce it.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Curator Choice Gallery Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Curator Choice Gallery Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Artwork set: empty chair, crowded street, broken cup, bright abstract pattern, family photo, dark landscape. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Curator Choice Gallery Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Curatorial themes: loss, modern life, beauty in ordinary objects, uncertainty, memory. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Curator Choice Gallery Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Curatorial themes: loss, modern life, beauty in ordinary objects, uncertainty, memory. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Curators do not only display knowledge in the arts; they help organize and produce it.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Curator Choice Gallery Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Curator Choice Gallery Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How do institutions shape knowledge in the arts?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Materials or Stimulus

- Six to eight artwork/image cards.
- Gallery floor-plan template and wall-label cards.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Artwork set: empty chair, crowded street, broken cup, bright abstract pattern, family photo, dark landscape.
- Curatorial themes: loss, modern life, beauty in ordinary objects, uncertainty, memory.

## Run Resources

- Gallery floor-plan template with numbered artwork spaces.
- Wall-label card: title, placement reason, connection to previous work.

## Activity Steps

1. Give groups the same artwork cards and a different curatorial theme.
2. Groups arrange the works in a sequence and write a title for the gallery.
3. Students add two wall labels explaining key choices.
4. Groups tour each gallery and record how the sequence changed meaning.
5. Debrief curation as interpretation, argument, and knowledge production.

## Teacher Language

- A gallery sequence is an argument the viewer walks through.
- What did your arrangement make the audience notice first?
