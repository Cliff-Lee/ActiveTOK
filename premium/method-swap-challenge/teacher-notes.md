# Method Swap Challenge Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students apply one AOK's method to another AOK's question and evaluate what breaks, transfers, or improves.

## TOK Fit

Category: Cross-AOK

Big idea: Methods carry assumptions about what can be known and how it should be justified.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Method Swap Challenge Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Method Swap Challenge Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Question: Is this policy fair? Method card: mathematical proof. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Method Swap Challenge Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Question: What caused this revolution? Method card: controlled experiment. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Method Swap Challenge Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Question: What caused this revolution? Method card: controlled experiment. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Methods carry assumptions about what can be known and how it should be justified.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Method Swap Challenge Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Method Swap Challenge Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How do methods shape what can be known?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how methods carry assumptions about what can be known and how it should be justified.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Question cards from different AOKs and method cards such as experiment, proof, interpretation, archive, survey, critique.
- Method-swap evaluation sheet.

## Setup Notes

- Prepare intentionally awkward pairings, such as proving a poem or interpreting a chemical reaction as art.
- Frame the first round as playful so students can see method assumptions clearly.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Question: Is this policy fair? Method card: mathematical proof.
- Question: What caused this revolution? Method card: controlled experiment.

## Run Resources

- Question cards, method cards, and mismatch reflection sheet.
- Evaluation grid: reveals, distorts, cannot answer, better method.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 35-45 min

| Stage | Teacher move | Facilitation note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Groups draw one knowledge question and one method from different AOKs. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 2 | They attempt to answer the question using the assigned method. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 3 | Groups identify what the method reveals, distorts, or cannot handle. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- A method is not just a tool; it carries a picture of what knowledge should look like.
- What did your method make impossible to see?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The proof method forced us to define fairness too narrowly.
- The archive method gave context but could not produce experimental control.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: provide obvious method matches after the mismatch round.
- Challenge: ask students to combine two methods and justify the combination.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students create their own method-swap card set for another class.
- Connect to interdisciplinary research and TOK essay comparisons.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How do methods shape what can be known?
- Can a method be reliable in one AOK and misleading in another?
- What is gained and lost when knowledge practices cross boundaries?

## Knowledge Questions

- How do methods shape what can be known?
- Are standards of justification transferable across AOKs?
- What does method choice reveal about knowledge?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A method-swap card pair showing an awkward method and a better-fitting method.
