# Perspective Glasses Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Perspective can limit and enrich knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Perspective Glasses Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Perspective Glasses Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Four role cards: scientist, artist, historian, and advertiser looking at one object. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Perspective Glasses Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: A classroom photo interpreted through safety, beauty, efficiency, and fairness lenses. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Perspective Glasses Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: A perspective switch where students must defend a reading they did not choose. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Perspective can limit and enrich knowledge.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Perspective Glasses Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Perspective Glasses Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Does perspective limit knowledge or enrich it?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Activity Steps

1. Assign groups different perspectives: student, teacher, parent, university admissions officer, AI company, another student.
2. Each group lists what they know, what they care about and what evidence they would request.
3. Groups present their interpretation.
4. The class separates factual disagreement from value disagreement.

## Teacher Language

- Write your first judgement before we discuss it; the first answer is useful evidence.
- Separate what you noticed from what you inferred.
- What would make this knowledge claim more reliable?
