# Mechanism Black Box Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students infer the mechanism inside a sealed system by testing inputs and outputs, then compare competing explanations.

## TOK Fit

Category: Natural Sciences

Big idea: Scientific explanation often goes beyond prediction by proposing mechanisms.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Mechanism Black Box Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Mechanism Black Box Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Rule box: input a number; output is double plus three. Competing mechanisms may fit early examples. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Mechanism Black Box Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Physical box: different objects go in; the teacher secretly applies a sorting rule before returning an output card. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Mechanism Black Box Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Physical box: different objects go in; the teacher secretly applies a sorting rule before returning an output card. Identify the observation, variable, control, anomaly, or missing method detail before making a claim.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Scientific explanation often goes beyond prediction by proposing mechanisms.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Mechanism Black Box Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Mechanism Black Box Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. What is added by knowing a mechanism?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how scientific explanation often goes beyond prediction by proposing mechanisms.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- A simple sealed black-box puzzle or teacher-controlled input-output rule.
- Mechanism proposal sheet.

## Setup Notes

- Use a hidden input-output rule that several mechanisms could initially explain.
- Prepare at least one test input that separates competing explanations.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Rule box: input a number; output is double plus three. Competing mechanisms may fit early examples.
- Physical box: different objects go in; the teacher secretly applies a sorting rule before returning an output card.

## Run Resources

- Input-output testing sheet.
- Mechanism diagram card with spaces for rule, prediction, and disconfirming test.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 30-40 min

| Stage | Teacher move | Facilitation note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Introduce a black box that produces outputs from inputs according to a hidden rule. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 2 | Groups test inputs and record outputs. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 3 | Students propose a mechanism that could explain the pattern. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 4 | Groups test a new input to discriminate between mechanisms. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 5 | Debrief why explanation requires more than curve-fitting. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| Debrief | Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. | Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- A pattern that predicts is useful, but a mechanism says why.
- What test would make one explanation fail?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- Two rules fit the first three inputs, so we needed a new test case.
- Our explanation became stronger when it predicted an output we had not seen.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use number rules with simple arithmetic.
- Challenge: include noisy data where one output is unreliable.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students compare mechanism in science with interpretation in history or arts.
- Ask groups to design a black box for another group to investigate.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: What is added by knowing a mechanism?
- Can a model predict well without explaining?
- How do scientists choose between competing unseen explanations?

## Knowledge Questions

- What is added by knowing a mechanism?
- Can prediction count as explanation?
- How is unseen scientific knowledge justified?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A black-box input-output table with two competing mechanism diagrams.
