# Facial Recognition Bias Audit Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students audit a fictional facial recognition system and decide what evidence would justify trusting or limiting it.

## TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and Technology

Big idea: Automated recognition is a knowledge claim whose reliability can vary across contexts and groups.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Facial Recognition Bias Audit Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Facial Recognition Bias Audit Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Audit row: hallway camera, 94% overall accuracy, but higher false positives in low light. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Facial Recognition Bias Audit Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Stakeholder card: student wrongly flagged, administrator managing safety, developer defending the model. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Facial Recognition Bias Audit Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Stakeholder card: student wrongly flagged, administrator managing safety, developer defending the model. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Automated recognition is a knowledge claim whose reliability can vary across contexts and groups.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Facial Recognition Bias Audit Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Facial Recognition Bias Audit Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. When is a technological classification reliable enough to act on?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how automated recognition is a knowledge claim whose reliability can vary across contexts and groups.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Fictional audit table with false positives and false negatives across groups.
- Stakeholder role cards and deployment decision sheet.

## Setup Notes

- Use fictional data and avoid using real student faces or sensitive biometric tools.
- Present the system as a claim-making tool: 'this face matches this identity.'

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Audit row: hallway camera, 94% overall accuracy, but higher false positives in low light.
- Stakeholder card: student wrongly flagged, administrator managing safety, developer defending the model.

## Run Resources

- Audit table with context, group, false positives, false negatives, and decision risk.
- Deployment verdict sheet: use, restrict, revise, reject, and evidence still needed.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- Overall accuracy is not the whole knowledge claim.
- Who bears the cost when a technological knower is wrong?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The system looked reliable until the errors were separated by context.
- We restricted use because the risk of false accusation outweighed convenience.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use two contexts and one error type.
- Challenge: ask groups to set a minimum evidence threshold for deployment.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students draft a transparency notice for any automated classification system.
- Compare algorithmic recognition with human eyewitness recognition.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: When is a technological classification reliable enough to act on?
- Can a system be accurate overall and still unjustified in practice?
- What evidence is needed before automated knowledge claims affect people?

## Knowledge Questions

- When is a technological classification reliable enough to act on?
- How should knowers weigh overall accuracy against uneven error?
- Who is accountable for knowledge produced by automated systems?
