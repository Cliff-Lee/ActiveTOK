# Invisible Gorilla Discussion Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students complete a selective-attention counting task and examine why obvious evidence can be missed.

## TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and the Knower

Big idea: Attention is not a neutral spotlight; it decides which evidence enters awareness.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Invisible Gorilla Discussion Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Invisible Gorilla Discussion Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Counting task: students count how many times a blue card is passed while a student in a bright scarf crosses the room. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Invisible Gorilla Discussion Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Desk task: students count red objects in a photo while a large background detail changes between views. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Invisible Gorilla Discussion Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Desk task: students count red objects in a photo while a large background detail changes between views. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Attention is not a neutral spotlight; it decides which evidence enters awareness.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Invisible Gorilla Discussion Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Invisible Gorilla Discussion Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How does attention shape what counts as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how attention is not a neutral spotlight; it decides which evidence enters awareness.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Short selective-attention video or teacher-created movement/counting task.
- Private answer slips and reveal slide.

## Setup Notes

- Use a short public-domain-style classroom task, or create your own with students passing a soft object while another visible change occurs.
- Tell students to focus on one countable task so the later miss is meaningful rather than random.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Counting task: students count how many times a blue card is passed while a student in a bright scarf crosses the room.
- Desk task: students count red objects in a photo while a large background detail changes between views.

## Run Resources

- Private count slip: target count, unexpected details noticed, confidence rating.
- Reveal slide with the unexpected element circled and a question about attention.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- You were not careless; your attention was doing what the task asked it to do.
- What did the task make available as evidence, and what did it make invisible?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- I counted accurately but missed the scarf because it was irrelevant to my task.
- The activity showed that focus improves one kind of knowledge while reducing another.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use one obvious unexpected feature.
- Challenge: add two unexpected features and ask which one different students notice.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students design a fairer observation protocol for an eyewitness or scientist.
- Compare selective attention in sport, medicine, driving, and classroom learning.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How does attention shape what counts as evidence?
- Can missing evidence be a rational consequence of focusing well?
- Where might expert attention improve knowledge and where might it narrow knowledge?

## Knowledge Questions

- How does attention shape what counts as evidence?
- To what extent is perception guided by expectation?
- Can expertise create blind spots as well as insight?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A still image from a selective-attention task with the missed feature highlighted.
