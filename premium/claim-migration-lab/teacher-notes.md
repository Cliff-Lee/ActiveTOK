# Claim Migration Lab Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students move one knowledge claim across several AOKs and watch how evidence standards and language must change.

## TOK Fit

Category: Cross-AOK

Big idea: A claim can change meaning and justification when it migrates between knowledge contexts.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Claim Migration Lab Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Claim Migration Lab Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Claim: 'This pattern is meaningful' in mathematics, natural sciences, arts, and history. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Claim Migration Lab Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Claim: 'This explanation is reliable' in human sciences, history, and technology. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Claim Migration Lab Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Claim: 'This explanation is reliable' in human sciences, history, and technology. Name the AOK, the method being used, and the standard of evidence you would apply.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — A claim can change meaning and justification when it migrates between knowledge contexts.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Claim Migration Lab Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Claim Migration Lab Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. What changes when a claim moves between AOKs?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how a claim can change meaning and justification when it migrates between knowledge contexts.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Claim cards that can travel across AOKs, such as 'This pattern is meaningful' or 'This explanation is reliable'.
- AOK translation grid.

## Setup Notes

- Choose claims with flexible wording so they can plausibly move between AOKs.
- Make students revise the claim, not just relabel the evidence.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Claim: 'This pattern is meaningful' in mathematics, natural sciences, arts, and history.
- Claim: 'This explanation is reliable' in human sciences, history, and technology.

## Run Resources

- AOK migration grid: claim wording, evidence standard, method, uncertainty, revised claim.
- AOK cards listing typical evidence and caution points.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 35-45 min

| Stage | Teacher move | Facilitation note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Choose one broad claim and place it in one AOK context. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 2 | Students identify what evidence would justify the claim there. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 3 | Move the same wording to a second and third AOK context. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 4 | Groups revise the claim so it fits each new context responsibly. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 5 | Debrief why claims cannot always travel unchanged. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| Debrief | Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. | Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- The same sentence may not make the same knowledge claim in a new AOK.
- What has to change so the claim becomes responsible in this context?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- In mathematics, meaningful meant provable structure; in the arts, it meant interpretive significance.
- We had to change 'reliable' into 'well-supported by archival evidence' for history.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use two AOKs and one claim.
- Challenge: require students to identify a term that cannot migrate safely.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students use one TOK essay claim and migrate it across two AOKs.
- Build a class glossary of terms that shift across knowledge contexts.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: What changes when a claim moves between AOKs?
- Are words like evidence, explanation, and reliability stable across knowledge contexts?
- How can interdisciplinary work avoid flattening differences?

## Knowledge Questions

- What changes when a claim moves between AOKs?
- Are standards of evidence stable across contexts?
- How should claims be translated across knowledge communities?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

One claim rewritten for mathematics, natural sciences, history, and the arts.
