# Infinity Hotel Check-In Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students act out Hilbert's Hotel to examine why mathematical infinity resists everyday intuition.

## TOK Fit

Category: Mathematics

Big idea: Mathematical concepts can be coherent even when they violate ordinary experience.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Infinity Hotel Check-In Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Infinity Hotel Check-In Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: One new guest arrives at a full infinite hotel; every current guest moves by $n → n + 1$. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Infinity Hotel Check-In Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Claim card: $∞ + 1 = ∞$ feels impossible until the rule is specified. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Infinity Hotel Check-In Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Claim card: $∞ + 1 = ∞$ feels impossible until the rule is specified. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Mathematical concepts can be coherent even when they violate ordinary experience.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Infinity Hotel Check-In Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Infinity Hotel Check-In Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Can mathematics know beyond intuition?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how mathematical concepts can be coherent even when they violate ordinary experience.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Numbered room cards.
- Guest cards.
- Move rule card showing $n → n + 1$ and claim card $∞ + 1 = ∞$.

## Setup Notes

- Use numbered cards on desks or around the room to represent hotel rooms.
- Make clear when students are reasoning about a formal model rather than a physically possible hotel.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- One new guest arrives at a full infinite hotel; every current guest moves by $n → n + 1$.
- Claim card: $∞ + 1 = ∞$ feels impossible until the rule is specified.

## Run Resources

- Room card set and move-rule card.
- Reflection sheet: what felt impossible, what the rule showed, and what the model assumes.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- Do not ask whether a real hotel could do this; ask whether the formal rule is coherent.
- Where did your everyday intuition stop helping?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The model was coherent even though I could not picture the whole hotel.
- I learned that mathematical possibility can depend on definitions rather than physical imagination.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: act out the first ten rooms only.
- Challenge: add infinitely many new guests by moving each current guest from room n to room 2n.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Compare countable infinity with a finite hotel and identify exactly where the rule fails.
- Ask whether mathematical objects need real-world counterparts to produce knowledge.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: Can mathematics produce knowledge about things we cannot imagine concretely?
- Why can a coherent formal system feel impossible?
- What is the role of intuition in mathematical knowledge?

## Knowledge Questions

- Can mathematics know beyond intuition?
- What makes a mathematical object meaningful?
- When should intuition be trusted in mathematics?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A numbered room card sequence illustrating the move rule $n → n + 1$.
