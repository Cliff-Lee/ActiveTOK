Recommendation Rabbit Hole Teacher Notes

Knowledge and Technology 30-40 min Premium draft

Students simulate repeated recommendations and observe how small choices can narrow a user's knowledge environment.

Back to Recommendation Rabbit Hole

Overview

Students simulate repeated recommendations and observe how small choices can narrow a user's knowledge environment.

TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and Technology

Big idea: A feed can become a path dependency: early clicks shape later knowledge possibilities.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

Student Prompt

Inspect Recommendation Rabbit Hole Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

Concrete Stimulus Packet

Stimulus

Recommendation Rabbit Hole Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Starting interest: exam stress; feed branches into study tips, productivity hacks, anxiety posts, tutoring adverts, and sleep science. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

Stimulus

Recommendation Rabbit Hole Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Starting interest: climate action; feed branches into science explainers, activism, conspiracy claims, policy debate, and lifestyle content. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

Stimulus

Recommendation Rabbit Hole Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Starting interest: climate action; feed branches into science explainers, activism, conspiracy claims, policy debate, and lifestyle content. Rank, sort, or audit this output. Identify which rule, ranking signal, or interface choice shaped your judgement.

Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — A feed can become a path dependency: early clicks shape later knowledge possibilities.

Actual Lesson Questions and Key

# Question for students Teacher key / cue
1 After inspecting Recommendation Rabbit Hole Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim? Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2 Which exact detail from Recommendation Rabbit Hole Stimulus A did you use as evidence? Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3 What did you infer beyond the evidence? Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4 How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%? Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5 What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim? Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6 When does personalization become distortion? Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how a feed can become a path dependency: early clicks shape later knowledge possibilities.

  • Strong response: separates observation from inference.
  • Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
  • Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
  • Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

Preparation

  • Topic cards, engagement tokens, and recommendation rule cards.
  • Path tracker showing each round of recommendations.

Setup Notes

  • Prepare enough cards that each round can narrow without becoming repetitive.
  • Keep content fictional and school-safe while preserving emotional and credibility differences.

Ready-to-Use Examples

  • Starting interest: exam stress; feed branches into study tips, productivity hacks, anxiety posts, tutoring adverts, and sleep science.
  • Starting interest: climate action; feed branches into science explainers, activism, conspiracy claims, policy debate, and lifestyle content.

Run Resources

  • Feed path tracker with rounds, clicked item, new recommendations, and perceived worldview.
  • Recommendation rule cards: similar topic, stronger emotion, most engaged, highest reliability.

Before Class

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

Suggested Timing

Total time: 30-40 min

Stage Teacher move Facilitation note
1 Give each student or group a starting interest card. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
2 Students choose one item from a small feed of mixed content. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
3 The algorithm adds more cards similar to the chosen card in the next round. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
4 Repeat for three or four rounds and map how feeds diverge. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
5 Debrief what users might come to believe is normal, popular, or true. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
Debrief Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective.

Sample Teacher Language

  • The user keeps choosing, but the menu of choices keeps changing.
  • At what point does personalization become a knowledge environment?

Facilitation Moves

  • Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
  • Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
  • Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
  • Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

Sample Student Output

  • My feed made the issue seem more extreme because each round selected stronger emotional content.
  • The algorithm did not lie, but it changed what I thought was common.

Likely Student Responses

  • Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
  • Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
  • Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
  • Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

Activity-Specific Adaptations

  • Support: run two rounds only.
  • Challenge: add engagement feedback so popular cards become more likely next round.

Extension Tasks

  • Students design an alternative feed rule that balances curiosity, reliability, and wellbeing.
  • Connect the path map to a TOK exhibition object such as a phone screen.

Assessment Evidence

  • Students distinguish observation from interpretation.
  • Students explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
  • Students formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
  • Students use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

Debrief Questions

  • Knowledge question: When does personalization become distortion?
  • Is a recommendation a neutral convenience or an argument about relevance?
  • Who is responsible for the knowledge environment created by a feed?

Knowledge Questions

  • When does personalization become distortion?
  • How do technological systems shape what knowers notice?
  • Can visibility be mistaken for importance?
