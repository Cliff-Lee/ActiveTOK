Anchoring Price Experiment Teacher Notes

Human Sciences 20-30 min Premium draft

Students estimate values after seeing different anchors and evaluate how irrelevant numbers shape judgement.

Back to Anchoring Price Experiment

Overview

Students estimate values after seeing different anchors and evaluate how irrelevant numbers shape judgement.

TOK Fit

Category: Human Sciences

Big idea: Human judgement can be pulled by context even when the context is not valid evidence.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

Student Prompt

Inspect Anchoring Price Experiment Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

Concrete Stimulus Packet

Stimulus

Anchoring Price Experiment Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: High anchor: Is this backpack worth more or less than $180? Estimate its value. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

Stimulus

Anchoring Price Experiment Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Low anchor: Is this backpack worth more or less than $25? Estimate its value. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

Stimulus

Anchoring Price Experiment Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Low anchor: Is this backpack worth more or less than $25? Estimate its value. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Human judgement can be pulled by context even when the context is not valid evidence.

Actual Lesson Questions and Key

# Question for students Teacher key / cue
1 After inspecting Anchoring Price Experiment Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim? Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2 Which exact detail from Anchoring Price Experiment Stimulus A did you use as evidence? Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3 What did you infer beyond the evidence? Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4 How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%? Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5 What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim? Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6 Can knowers fully explain their own judgements? Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how human judgement can be pulled by context even when the context is not valid evidence.

  • Strong response: separates observation from inference.
  • Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
  • Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
  • Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

Preparation

  • Two versions of an estimate sheet with different anchor numbers.
  • Common objects or scenarios for valuation.

Setup Notes

  • Randomly distribute high-anchor and low-anchor sheets without announcing the difference.
  • Use estimates where students are unlikely to know the exact answer.

Ready-to-Use Examples

  • High anchor: Is this backpack worth more or less than $180? Estimate its value.
  • Low anchor: Is this backpack worth more or less than $25? Estimate its value.

Run Resources

  • Two estimate sheet versions with anchor prompts.
  • Class results table comparing group averages and spread.

Before Class

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

Suggested Timing

Total time: 20-30 min

Stage Teacher move Facilitation note
1 Randomly give students a high-anchor or low-anchor version of the sheet. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
2 Students answer whether the true value is higher or lower than the anchor. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
3 Students then give their best estimate. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
4 Compare average estimates across anchor groups. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
5 Debrief why irrelevant numbers can influence judgement. Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims.
Debrief Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective.

Sample Teacher Language

  • The anchor was not evidence, but it still entered the judgement.
  • How could a human scientist show that the context caused a difference?

Facilitation Moves

  • Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
  • Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
  • Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
  • Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

Sample Student Output

  • The high-anchor group gave higher estimates even though we saw the same object.
  • I did not think the anchor affected me until the class averages were compared.

Likely Student Responses

  • Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
  • Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
  • Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
  • Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

Activity-Specific Adaptations

  • Support: use one object and two anchor sheets.
  • Challenge: add a no-anchor control group.

Extension Tasks

  • Students design a follow-up experiment with random assignment and a control group.
  • Connect anchoring to pricing, negotiation, sentencing, or predicted grades.

Assessment Evidence

  • Students distinguish observation from interpretation.
  • Students explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
  • Students formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
  • Students use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

Debrief Questions

  • Knowledge question: What does bias reveal about human judgement?
  • Can we know our own reasons for a decision?
  • How do human scientists design studies to detect hidden influences?

Knowledge Questions

  • Can knowers fully explain their own judgements?
  • How can experiments reveal hidden influences?
  • When does context become evidence and when is it bias?
