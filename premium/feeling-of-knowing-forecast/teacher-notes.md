# Feeling of Knowing Forecast Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students predict which forgotten answers they would recognize later, then test the reliability of that feeling.

## TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and the Knower

Big idea: The feeling that knowledge is available is itself a judgement that can be tested.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Feeling of Knowing Forecast Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Feeling of Knowing Forecast Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Recall prompt: What is the term for a word that imitates a sound?. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Feeling of Knowing Forecast Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Recognition options later: metaphor, onomatopoeia, alliteration, oxymoron. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Feeling of Knowing Forecast Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Recognition options later: metaphor, onomatopoeia, alliteration, oxymoron. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — The feeling that knowledge is available is itself a judgement that can be tested.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Feeling of Knowing Forecast Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Feeling of Knowing Forecast Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How reliable is introspection as evidence about what we know?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how the feeling that knowledge is available is itself a judgement that can be tested.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Ten recall questions, matching recognition options, and a forecast table.
- Timer and answer key.

## Setup Notes

- Use questions students might plausibly know but may not immediately retrieve.
- Make students forecast recognition before seeing options so the metacognitive judgement is visible.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Recall prompt: What is the term for a word that imitates a sound?
- Recognition options later: metaphor, onomatopoeia, alliteration, oxymoron.

## Run Resources

- Forecast table with columns: no recall, feeling-of-knowing rating, recognition answer, outcome.
- Class tally comparing high forecast items and actual recognition success.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- A feeling of knowing is useful data, but it is not the same as knowing.
- What kind of evidence could confirm or challenge your feeling of familiarity?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- I was sure I would recognize the answer, but all the options felt familiar.
- My forecast was strongest when I could remember the topic area, not just the feeling.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use fewer questions and simple recognition options.
- Challenge: separate feeling-of-knowing, confidence, and source memory ratings.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students compare feeling-of-knowing with confidence calibration data.
- Ask students to create a revision strategy based on metacognitive evidence.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How reliable is introspection as evidence about what we know?
- What is the difference between not knowing and not retrieving?
- When should a knower trust a feeling of familiarity?

## Knowledge Questions

- How reliable is introspection as evidence about what we know?
- Does feeling familiar make a claim more justified?
- How can knowers test their own reliability?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A forecast table comparing feeling-of-knowing ratings with recognition results.
