# Framing Risk Choice Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students make choices under gain and loss frames, then compare how wording changes apparently rational decisions.

## TOK Fit

Category: Human Sciences

Big idea: The same evidence can lead to different choices when framed as gain or loss.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Framing Risk Choice Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Framing Risk Choice Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Gain frame: 200 of 600 people will be saved for sure, or a one-third chance all 600 are saved. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Framing Risk Choice Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Loss frame: 400 of 600 people will die for sure, or a one-third chance nobody dies. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Framing Risk Choice Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Loss frame: 400 of 600 people will die for sure, or a one-third chance nobody dies. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — The same evidence can lead to different choices when framed as gain or loss.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Framing Risk Choice Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Framing Risk Choice Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Are decisions based on evidence, framing, or both?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how the same evidence can lead to different choices when framed as gain or loss.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Two versions of a risk-choice scenario: gain frame and loss frame.
- Anonymous response slips and class tally table.

## Setup Notes

- Prepare mathematically equivalent gain-frame and loss-frame versions.
- Collect anonymous choices so students examine patterns, not individual judgement.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Gain frame: 200 of 600 people will be saved for sure, or a one-third chance all 600 are saved.
- Loss frame: 400 of 600 people will die for sure, or a one-third chance nobody dies.

## Run Resources

- Two scenario sheets with equivalent outcomes and different wording.
- Class tally table comparing safe/risky choices under each frame.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 20-30 min

| Stage | Teacher move | Facilitation note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Randomly give half the class a gain-framed version and half a loss-framed version. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 2 | Students choose between a certain option and a risky option. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 3 | Collect anonymous choices and compare class patterns by frame. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 4 | Reveal that the underlying outcomes were equivalent. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 5 | Debrief how framing can shape judgement without changing facts. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| Debrief | Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. | Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- The numbers did not change; the frame did.
- If wording changes choices, what does that reveal about evidence and emotion?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- I chose the safe option when it was framed as saving people but risked more when it was framed as deaths.
- The same data felt different because the loss frame made inaction feel worse.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use smaller numbers and draw the outcomes.
- Challenge: include a third neutral-frame condition.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students identify gain/loss framing in public health, climate, or school policy messages.
- Design a more transparent risk communication statement.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: Are choices based on evidence, framing, or both?
- Can a rational decision be separated from how options are described?
- How do human scientists study hidden influences on judgement?

## Knowledge Questions

- Are decisions based on evidence, framing, or both?
- How does language shape human judgement?
- What does experimental evidence reveal about rationality?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

Two equivalent risk scenarios, one framed as lives saved and one as lives lost.
