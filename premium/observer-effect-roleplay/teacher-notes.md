# Observer Effect Roleplay Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students roleplay a classroom observation study and examine how being observed changes behavior and evidence.

## TOK Fit

Category: Human Sciences

Big idea: In human sciences, observation can become part of the situation being observed.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Observer Effect Roleplay Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Observer Effect Roleplay Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Task: build the tallest paper tower in five minutes while observers visibly tally leadership, interruptions, and suggestions. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Observer Effect Roleplay Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Second round: observers sit farther away and use a simpler tally scheme. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Observer Effect Roleplay Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Second round: observers sit farther away and use a simpler tally scheme. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — In human sciences, observation can become part of the situation being observed.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Observer Effect Roleplay Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Observer Effect Roleplay Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Can observation alter what is known?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how in human sciences, observation can become part of the situation being observed.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Observer role cards, participant role cards, and behavior tally sheets.
- Simple group task such as puzzle-solving or discussion.

## Setup Notes

- Make the observation visible and ethical; students should know the activity studies observation effects.
- Use a harmless group task where behavior can naturally vary.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Task: build the tallest paper tower in five minutes while observers visibly tally leadership, interruptions, and suggestions.
- Second round: observers sit farther away and use a simpler tally scheme.

## Run Resources

- Behavior coding sheet with three visible behaviors.
- Participant reflection card: what changed because you knew you were observed?

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Sample Teacher Language

- In human sciences, the method can enter the behavior it measures.
- Which version gave cleaner data, and which version was more natural?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- I spoke more carefully because I saw someone tallying interruptions.
- The visible observation made the data easier to collect but less natural.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use two coded behaviors.
- Challenge: compare observer agreement between two coders.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students redesign the observation to improve validity while preserving consent.
- Connect to classroom observations, workplace studies, and ethnography.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: Can human behavior be observed without changing it?
- How should researchers balance natural behavior, consent, and control?
- What counts as good evidence when people react to being studied?

## Knowledge Questions

- Can observation alter what is known?
- How do ethics shape methods in human sciences?
- What makes behavioral evidence reliable?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

Two observation tally sheets from visible and less visible observation conditions.
