# Restoration Ethics Studio Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students decide how to restore a damaged artwork and analyze how restoration choices affect authenticity and knowledge.

## TOK Fit

Category: The Arts

Big idea: Preserving an artwork can also alter what future viewers know through it.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Scenario: a mural has faded paint, later graffiti, and a damaged corner. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Options: clean only, repaint invisibly, preserve damage, add a modern label, commission a visible repair. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Restoration Ethics Studio Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Options: clean only, repaint invisibly, preserve damage, add a modern label, commission a visible repair. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Preserving an artwork can also alter what future viewers know through it.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Does restoration preserve knowledge or create new meaning?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how preserving an artwork can also alter what future viewers know through it.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Damaged artwork scenario card or image description.
- Restoration option cards: leave, clean, repair visibly, repair invisibly, reinterpret.

## Setup Notes

- Use a fictional artwork so students can focus on trade-offs rather than external facts.
- Give each group a different stakeholder priority.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Scenario: a mural has faded paint, later graffiti, and a damaged corner.
- Options: clean only, repaint invisibly, preserve damage, add a modern label, commission a visible repair.

## Run Resources

- Restoration decision matrix: authenticity, artist intention, public access, historical trace, reversibility.
- Stakeholder cards: artist estate, museum, local community, conservator, visitor.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

Total time: 30-40 min

## Sample Teacher Language

- Preservation is not neutral when every option changes what viewers encounter.
- What kind of authenticity are you prioritizing?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- Invisible repair preserved the image but hid the artwork's history.
- Leaving the damage visible treated the object as historical evidence, not just aesthetic experience.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use three restoration options.
- Challenge: include a budget constraint and public controversy.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students write a museum label explaining a restoration decision transparently.
- Compare restoration with editing a historical source or cleaning scientific data.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: Does restoration preserve knowledge or create a new object of knowledge?
- What counts as authenticity in the arts?
- Who should decide how an artwork is preserved?

## Knowledge Questions

- Does restoration preserve knowledge or create new meaning?
- What counts as authenticity in the arts?
- How do values shape artistic knowledge?
