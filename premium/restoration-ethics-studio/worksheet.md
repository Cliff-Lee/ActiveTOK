# Restoration Ethics Studio Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Preserving an artwork can also alter what future viewers know through it.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Scenario: a mural has faded paint, later graffiti, and a damaged corner. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Options: clean only, repaint invisibly, preserve damage, add a modern label, commission a visible repair. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Restoration Ethics Studio Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Options: clean only, repaint invisibly, preserve damage, add a modern label, commission a visible repair. Describe what you see or hear first, then add title/context and revise the interpretation.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Preserving an artwork can also alter what future viewers know through it.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Restoration Ethics Studio Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Does restoration preserve knowledge or create new meaning?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Materials or Stimulus

- Damaged artwork scenario card or image description.
- Restoration option cards: leave, clean, repair visibly, repair invisibly, reinterpret.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Scenario: a mural has faded paint, later graffiti, and a damaged corner.
- Options: clean only, repaint invisibly, preserve damage, add a modern label, commission a visible repair.

## Run Resources

- Restoration decision matrix: authenticity, artist intention, public access, historical trace, reversibility.
- Stakeholder cards: artist estate, museum, local community, conservator, visitor.

## Activity Steps

1. Present a fictional damaged artwork and its background.
2. Groups inspect restoration options and stakeholder priorities.
3. Students choose a restoration plan and justify it with evidence and values.
4. Groups compare how each plan changes authenticity and viewer knowledge.
5. Debrief whether preservation can be a form of interpretation.

## Teacher Language

- Preservation is not neutral when every option changes what viewers encounter.
- What kind of authenticity are you prioritizing?
