# Interviewer Effect Simulation Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students conduct mini-interviews with different interviewer styles and compare how method shapes responses.

## TOK Fit

Category: Human Sciences

Big idea: In human sciences, the person asking can become part of the evidence produced.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Interviewer Effect Simulation Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Interviewer Effect Simulation Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Question: 'How useful is homework for learning?' Styles: warm, rushed, leading, skeptical, neutral. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Interviewer Effect Simulation Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Question: 'What makes a classroom discussion good?' Styles: encouraging, distracted, formal. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Interviewer Effect Simulation Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Question: 'What makes a classroom discussion good?' Styles: encouraging, distracted, formal. Predict how people will respond, then identify the wording, context, incentive, or role that could change the result.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — In human sciences, the person asking can become part of the evidence produced.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Interviewer Effect Simulation Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Interviewer Effect Simulation Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How can data be shaped by the method used to collect it?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how in human sciences, the person asking can become part of the evidence produced.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Interview question cards and interviewer style cards.
- Response comparison sheet.

## Setup Notes

- Use safe topics such as homework routines, cafeteria preferences, or study environments.
- Assign interviewer styles openly so students can analyze method rather than feel manipulated.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Question: 'How useful is homework for learning?' Styles: warm, rushed, leading, skeptical, neutral.
- Question: 'What makes a classroom discussion good?' Styles: encouraging, distracted, formal.

## Run Resources

- Interview style cards.
- Response comparison sheet: length, detail, hesitation, confidence, interviewer influence.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 30-40 min

| Stage | Teacher move | Facilitation note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Pairs receive the same interview question but different interviewer style cards. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 2 | Students conduct short, low-stakes interviews. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 3 | Respondents reflect on how the style affected their answer. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 4 | Groups compare differences in length, confidence, and content. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |

| Debrief | Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. | Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- The question is not the only method; the social situation is part of the method.
- How did the interviewer change what felt safe or relevant to say?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The leading interviewer got shorter answers that agreed more quickly.
- The warm interviewer produced more detail, but maybe also more desire to please.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use two interviewer styles.
- Challenge: add inter-rater coding of response detail.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students design a better interview protocol with ethics notes.
- Compare interviews with surveys, observation, and experiments.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How can data be shaped by the method used to collect it?
- Can a neutral interview ever be fully neutral?
- What does ethical interviewing require?

## Knowledge Questions

- How can data be shaped by the method used to collect it?
- What makes interview evidence reliable?
- How do ethics and method interact in human sciences?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

Two interview transcripts from the same question but different interviewer styles.
