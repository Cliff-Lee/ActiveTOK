# Ambiguity Court Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students put ambiguous statements on trial and decide which interpretations are reasonable, strained, or unsupported.

## TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and Language

Big idea: Ambiguity is not just confusion; it reveals how context and assumptions complete meaning.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Ambiguity Court Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Ambiguity Court Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Sentence: 'I saw her duck.' Context A: a birdwatching trip. Context B: a football flying across the room. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Ambiguity Court Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Sentence: 'The teacher said the student was brilliant with a smile.'. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Ambiguity Court Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Sentence: 'The teacher said the student was brilliant with a smile.'. Annotate the wording. Circle the words that change tone, emotion, implication, or trust.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Ambiguity is not just confusion; it reveals how context and assumptions complete meaning.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Ambiguity Court Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Ambiguity Court Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How much meaning is in words and how much is in context?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how ambiguity is not just confusion; it reveals how context and assumptions complete meaning.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Ambiguous statement cards and context cards.
- Verdict sheet: reasonable, possible but weak, unsupported.

## Setup Notes

- Choose sentences that are genuinely ambiguous but not socially risky.
- Add context gradually so students can see interpretation change in stages.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Sentence: 'I saw her duck.' Context A: a birdwatching trip. Context B: a football flying across the room.
- Sentence: 'The teacher said the student was brilliant with a smile.'

## Run Resources

- Ambiguity verdict sheet: interpretation, supporting evidence, context needed, verdict.
- Context cards that add speaker, setting, tone, and purpose.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 25-35 min

| 1 | Give groups an ambiguous sentence with no context. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- Possible does not mean equally justified.
- What evidence moved an interpretation from possible to reasonable?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The words allowed both meanings, but the context made one much better supported.
- I realized I was adding tone that was not actually in the sentence.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: start with visual puns or simple ambiguous sentences.
- Challenge: distinguish semantic ambiguity, syntactic ambiguity, and pragmatic ambiguity.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students find ambiguity in a headline, policy, poem, or translated phrase.
- Connect ambiguity to legal interpretation or literary criticism.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How much meaning is in the words and how much is supplied by context?
- When is an interpretation justified rather than merely possible?
- Can ambiguity be useful for knowledge?

## Knowledge Questions

- How much meaning is in words and how much is in context?
- What makes an interpretation justified?
- Can ambiguous language produce knowledge?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

An ambiguous sentence annotated with three possible interpretations and one context card.
