# Emotion Snapshot Audit Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students interpret the same ambiguous scene under different emotional states and compare how feeling directs attention.

## TOK Fit

Category: Knowledge and the Knower

Big idea: Emotion is not merely noise in knowing; it can focus, filter, and frame evidence.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Scene: a student sees two friends whispering and laughing near the classroom door. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Emotion frames: anxious, excited, suspicious, calm, embarrassed, proud. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Emotion Snapshot Audit Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Emotion frames: anxious, excited, suspicious, calm, embarrassed, proud. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Emotion is not merely noise in knowing; it can focus, filter, and frame evidence.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How does emotion shape what knowers notice?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how emotion is not merely noise in knowing; it can focus, filter, and frame evidence.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Ambiguous image or short written scene.
- Emotion frame cards such as anxious, excited, suspicious, calm, proud, embarrassed.

## Setup Notes

- Use fictional emotion cards rather than asking students to disclose their actual mood.
- Choose an ambiguous image or short scene with several plausible interpretations.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Scene: a student sees two friends whispering and laughing near the classroom door.
- Emotion frames: anxious, excited, suspicious, calm, embarrassed, proud.

## Run Resources

- Emotion frame cards with noticing prompts.
- Interpretation grid: detail noticed, inference, emotion frame, evidence strength.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 20-30 min

## Sample Teacher Language

- Emotion did not add new visual evidence, but it changed which details mattered.
- When might emotion help you notice something important?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- The suspicious frame made the whispering seem hostile, while the excited frame made it seem like a surprise.
- The same detail supported different interpretations because the emotional frame changed.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: use two emotion frames only.
- Challenge: ask students to identify when emotion provides relevant evidence.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students compare emotion in eyewitness testimony, art interpretation, and ethical judgement.
- Create a checking routine for emotionally charged knowledge claims.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: How does emotion shape what knowers notice?
- Can emotion improve knowledge by making some evidence salient?
- When does emotional interpretation become distortion?

## Knowledge Questions

- How does emotion shape what knowers notice?
- Can emotion be a source of insight as well as bias?
- When should emotional evidence be checked by other methods?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

One ambiguous image annotated twice from two emotional frames.
