# Emotion Snapshot Audit Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Emotion is not merely noise in knowing; it can focus, filter, and frame evidence.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Scene: a student sees two friends whispering and laughing near the classroom door. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: Emotion frames: anxious, excited, suspicious, calm, embarrassed, proud. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Emotion Snapshot Audit Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: Emotion frames: anxious, excited, suspicious, calm, embarrassed, proud. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Emotion is not merely noise in knowing; it can focus, filter, and frame evidence.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Emotion Snapshot Audit Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How does emotion shape what knowers notice?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Materials or Stimulus

- Ambiguous image or short written scene.
- Emotion frame cards such as anxious, excited, suspicious, calm, proud, embarrassed.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Scene: a student sees two friends whispering and laughing near the classroom door.
- Emotion frames: anxious, excited, suspicious, calm, embarrassed, proud.

## Run Resources

- Emotion frame cards with noticing prompts.
- Interpretation grid: detail noticed, inference, emotion frame, evidence strength.

## Activity Steps

1. Show students the same ambiguous stimulus without discussion.
2. Assign groups different emotion frame cards.
3. Groups list what details they notice, what they infer, and what they ignore.
4. Compare interpretations across emotion frames.
5. Debrief emotion as evidence, filter, and interpretive context.

## Teacher Language

- Emotion did not add new visual evidence, but it changed which details mattered.
- When might emotion help you notice something important?
