# Eyewitness Trap Worksheet

Name: ________________________________  Class: __________________  Date: __________________

## Learning Focus

Memory is reconstructive, not photographic.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Eyewitness Trap Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Eyewitness Trap Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: A busy classroom image followed by questions about colours, positions, and text. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Eyewitness Trap Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: A short silent hallway scene with one misleading follow-up question. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Eyewitness Trap Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: A confidence-versus-accuracy table for memory claims. Before discussing, write what you noticed, what you inferred, and how confident you are.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Memory is reconstructive, not photographic.


## Actual Lesson Questions

1. After inspecting Eyewitness Trap Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Eyewitness Trap Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. When should memory be trusted as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Before the Activity

1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

## Activity Steps

1. Show a busy image for 10 seconds.
2. Remove it and ask factual questions: colours, objects, positions, text, number of people.
3. Ask students to rate their confidence in each answer.
4. Reveal the image and compare accuracy with confidence.

## Teacher Language

- Write your first judgement before we discuss it; the first answer is useful evidence.
- Separate what you noticed from what you inferred.
- What would make this knowledge claim more reliable?
