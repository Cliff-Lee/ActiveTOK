Correlation Illusion Cards Worksheet

Mathematics 25-35 min Premium draft

Students sort paired-variable cards and discover how small samples and salient cases create illusory correlations.

Back to Correlation Illusion Cards

Learning Focus

Patterns can feel meaningful before they are mathematically justified.

Student Prompt

Inspect Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

Concrete Stimulus Packet

Stimulus

Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Cards pair 'used flashcards / did not use flashcards' with 'high quiz score / lower quiz score'. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

Stimulus

Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: A few vivid cards show flashcards plus high scores, while the full set is mixed. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

Stimulus

Correlation Illusion Cards Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: A few vivid cards show flashcards plus high scores, while the full set is mixed. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Patterns can feel meaningful before they are mathematically justified.

Actual Lesson Questions

# Question for students Teacher cue
1 After inspecting Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim? Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2 Which exact detail from Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus A did you use as evidence? Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3 What did you infer beyond the evidence? Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4 How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%? Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5 What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim? Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6 When does a perceived pattern become evidence? Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

Before the Activity

  1. What do you think this activity will test about knowledge, evidence, or interpretation?
  2. What assumptions might you bring to this activity?
  3. How confident are you that your first judgement will be reliable? Explain briefly.

Materials or Stimulus

  • Paired cards showing two variables, such as study method and quiz result or weather and mood.
  • Four-cell contingency table.

Ready-to-Use Examples

  • Cards pair 'used flashcards / did not use flashcards' with 'high quiz score / lower quiz score'.
  • A few vivid cards show flashcards plus high scores, while the full set is mixed.

Run Resources

  • Four-cell table mat for sorting all pairings.
  • Salience marker: students star the cards they remembered before counting.

Activity Steps

  1. Show a small set of memorable paired examples and ask students to guess the relationship.
  2. Give groups the full card set to sort into a four-cell table.
  3. Students count all cells, not just confirming cases.
  4. Groups decide whether the perceived relationship survives counting.
  5. Debrief why humans notice some pairings more than others.

Teacher Language

  • A memorable case is not automatically representative evidence.
  • Which cell of the table did your intuition ignore?

Student Task

Use the stimulus cards to complete the observation/inference/evidence/confidence grid, then answer one TOK knowledge question connected to Mathematics.

TOK Debrief

  • Knowledge question: When does a perceived pattern become reliable mathematical evidence?
  • Why are confirming cases often more memorable than disconfirming cases?
  • How should knowers protect themselves from pattern hunger?

Knowledge Questions

  • When does a perceived pattern become evidence?
  • How do salience and sample size affect knowledge?
  • Can counting change what seems obvious?

Choose one question above and write a 5-7 sentence response that uses this activity as evidence.
