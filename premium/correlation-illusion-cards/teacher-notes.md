# Correlation Illusion Cards Teacher Notes

## Overview

Students sort paired-variable cards and discover how small samples and salient cases create illusory correlations.

## TOK Fit

Category: Mathematics

Big idea: Patterns can feel meaningful before they are mathematically justified.

This activity works best when students first experience a small uncertainty, surprise, disagreement, or interpretive tension, then name what the experience reveals about knowledge.

## Student Prompt

Inspect Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus A. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus A

Student-facing stimulus 1: Cards pair 'used flashcards / did not use flashcards' with 'high quiz score / lower quiz score'. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Students inspect this exact card first and commit to a claim before hearing anyone else.

### Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus B

Student-facing stimulus 2: A few vivid cards show flashcards plus high scores, while the full set is mixed. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Use this for comparison, a second round, or a partner challenge.

### Correlation Illusion Cards Reveal / Twist

Student-facing stimulus 3: A few vivid cards show flashcards plus high scores, while the full set is mixed. Work the example, then decide whether it gives a pattern, a model, a calculation, or a proof.

Teacher cue: Teacher reveal: connect the changed response to the big idea — Patterns can feel meaningful before they are mathematically justified.


## Actual Lesson Questions and Key

1. After inspecting Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus A, what is your first knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. Which exact detail from Correlation Illusion Cards Stimulus A did you use as evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. When does a perceived pattern become evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Teacher Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how patterns can feel meaningful before they are mathematically justified.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Preparation

- Paired cards showing two variables, such as study method and quiz result or weather and mood.
- Four-cell contingency table.

## Setup Notes

- Build card sets where confirming cases are memorable but not actually dominant.
- Ask for a gut judgement before students count the full table.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Cards pair 'used flashcards / did not use flashcards' with 'high quiz score / lower quiz score'.
- A few vivid cards show flashcards plus high scores, while the full set is mixed.

## Run Resources

- Four-cell table mat for sorting all pairings.
- Salience marker: students star the cards they remembered before counting.

Before class, prepare the stimulus or scenario and decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.

## Suggested Timing

Total time: 25-35 min

| Stage | Teacher move | Facilitation note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Show a small set of memorable paired examples and ask students to guess the relationship. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 2 | Give groups the full card set to sort into a four-cell table. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 3 | Students count all cells, not just confirming cases. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 4 | Groups decide whether the perceived relationship survives counting. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| 5 | Debrief why humans notice some pairings more than others. | Watch for student assumptions, confidence shifts, and evidence claims. |
| Debrief | Use the TOK questions to move from activity outcome to knowledge analysis. | Ask students to distinguish evidence, interpretation, confidence, and perspective. |

## Sample Teacher Language

- A memorable case is not automatically representative evidence.
- Which cell of the table did your intuition ignore?

## Facilitation Moves

- Start with private thinking before public discussion so students can notice their own assumptions.
- Ask students to justify claims with evidence from the activity rather than general opinion.
- Press for TOK language: evidence, justification, perspective, method, reliability, interpretation, certainty, and community.
- Keep the tone low-stakes; the goal is reflective noticing, not catching students out.

## Sample Student Output

- I remembered the flashcard success stories and forgot the non-flashcard success stories.
- Counting all four cells weakened the pattern I thought I saw.

## Likely Student Responses

- Students may treat the activity as a trick rather than as evidence about knowing.
- Students may overgeneralise from one classroom demonstration.
- Students may say "it depends" without naming the criteria that matter.
- Students may need help turning a concrete result into an abstract knowledge question.

## Common Pitfalls

- Moving too quickly to explanation before students record their first judgement.
- Letting the discussion become only psychological content rather than TOK analysis.
- Treating uncertainty as failure instead of as a normal feature of inquiry.

## Activity-Specific Adaptations

- Support: color-code the four table cells.
- Challenge: ask students to estimate strength of association before and after counting.

## Differentiation

- Support: Give students sentence starters such as "This shows that knowledge depends on..." or "The evidence is limited because...".
- Challenge: Ask students to compare this activity with another AOK or design a stronger method for testing the same issue.
- Extension: Have students turn one debrief point into a TOK exhibition-style object commentary.

## Extension Tasks

- Students collect a harmless class dataset and test for perceived versus counted patterns.
- Connect to stereotypes, health claims, or study advice.

## Assessment Evidence

Look for students who can:

- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Explain how a method, tool, language choice, or perspective shapes knowledge.
- Formulate a knowledge question rather than a subject-specific factual question.
- Use the classroom activity as evidence without overclaiming.

## Debrief Questions

- Knowledge question: When does a perceived pattern become reliable mathematical evidence?
- Why are confirming cases often more memorable than disconfirming cases?
- How should knowers protect themselves from pattern hunger?

## Knowledge Questions

- When does a perceived pattern become evidence?
- How do salience and sample size affect knowledge?
- Can counting change what seems obvious?

## Exhibition Object Suggestion

A four-cell table showing confirming and disconfirming examples.
