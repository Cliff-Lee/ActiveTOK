## Purpose

Students examine how visual scale affects judgements about causation, continuity, and change.

Use timelines to show that historical representation is an argument about significance.

## Materials

- Event cards for a long-term historical process.
- Blank timeline strips with different scales.

## Setup Notes

- Use an event sequence with both long-term causes and sudden triggers.

- History example: reform movement builds slowly before one visible law or protest.

## Student Prompt

Inspect the Timeline Compression Trap stimulus. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Actual Lesson Questions

1. What is your first knowledge claim after inspecting the Timeline Compression Trap stimulus?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. What specific evidence did you use?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How do historical representations shape interpretation?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Student Task

Use the stimulus cards to complete the observation/inference/evidence/confidence grid, then answer one TOK knowledge question connected to History.

## Teacher Key / Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how the scale of a timeline shapes historical interpretation.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Procedure

1. Give groups the same set of event cards.
2. One group uses a compressed timeline and another uses an expanded timeline.
3. Students place events and write a one-sentence interpretation of the process.
4. Groups compare whether change looks sudden, gradual, inevitable, or contested.
5. Debrief how representation affects historical knowledge.

## Teacher Language

- Your timeline is making an argument about pace and importance.
- What disappears when the scale changes?

## TOK Debrief

- Knowledge question: How do historical representations shape interpretation?
- Does changing scale change what counts as a cause?
- When does simplification become distortion?

## Knowledge Questions

- How do historical representations shape interpretation?
- What does timeline scale reveal or hide?
- When does simplification distort knowledge?

## Exhibition Object Idea

Two timelines of the same events drawn at different scales.

## Sample Student Output

- The compressed version made the change look sudden and inevitable.
- The expanded version showed earlier warnings and failed attempts.

## Adaptations

- Support: use six event cards.
- Challenge: require students to mark continuity, change, and turning points separately.

## Extension Tasks

- Students add a third timeline focused on one actor's perspective.
- Connect timeline scale to causation essays in history.

## Teacher Notes

This works with any unit involving long-term change.

Ask students to identify what their timeline makes visually persuasive.
