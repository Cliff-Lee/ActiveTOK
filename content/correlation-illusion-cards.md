## Purpose

Students compare perceived association with counted evidence.

Use card sorting to show why salient examples can dominate statistical judgement.

## Materials

- Paired cards showing two variables, such as study method and quiz result or weather and mood.
- Four-cell contingency table.

## Setup Notes

- Build card sets where confirming cases are memorable but not actually dominant.
- Ask for a gut judgement before students count the full table.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Cards pair 'used flashcards / did not use flashcards' with 'high quiz score / lower quiz score'.
- A few vivid cards show flashcards plus high scores, while the full set is mixed.

## Run Resources

- Four-cell table mat for sorting all pairings.
- Salience marker: students star the cards they remembered before counting.

## Student Prompt

Inspect the Correlation Illusion Cards stimulus. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Actual Lesson Questions

1. What is your first knowledge claim after inspecting the Correlation Illusion Cards stimulus?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. What specific evidence did you use?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. When does a perceived pattern become evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Pattern or Proof Card

Sequence A: 2, 4, 8, 16, 32. Student claim: “The next number must be 64.” Alternative rule: double until 32, then subtract 10. Both fit the visible pattern so far.

Teacher cue: Use this to separate pattern confidence from proof or model choice.

### Correlation Illusion Cards Example

Cards pair 'used flashcards / did not use flashcards' with 'high quiz score / lower quiz score'.

Teacher cue: Use this as the activity-specific version of the stimulus.

### Comparison / Twist Card

A few vivid cards show flashcards plus high scores, while the full set is mixed.

Teacher cue: Reveal after students commit to their first judgement.


## Student Task

Use the stimulus cards to complete the observation/inference/evidence/confidence grid, then answer one TOK knowledge question connected to Mathematics.

## Teacher Key / Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how patterns can feel meaningful before they are mathematically justified.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Procedure

1. Show a small set of memorable paired examples and ask students to guess the relationship.
2. Give groups the full card set to sort into a four-cell table.
3. Students count all cells, not just confirming cases.
4. Groups decide whether the perceived relationship survives counting.
5. Debrief why humans notice some pairings more than others.

## Teacher Language

- A memorable case is not automatically representative evidence.
- Which cell of the table did your intuition ignore?

## TOK Debrief

- Knowledge question: When does a perceived pattern become reliable mathematical evidence?
- Why are confirming cases often more memorable than disconfirming cases?
- How should knowers protect themselves from pattern hunger?

## Knowledge Questions

- When does a perceived pattern become evidence?
- How do salience and sample size affect knowledge?
- Can counting change what seems obvious?

## Exhibition Object Idea

A four-cell table showing confirming and disconfirming examples.

## Sample Student Output

- I remembered the flashcard success stories and forgot the non-flashcard success stories.
- Counting all four cells weakened the pattern I thought I saw.

## Adaptations

- Support: color-code the four table cells.
- Challenge: ask students to estimate strength of association before and after counting.

## Extension Tasks

- Students collect a harmless class dataset and test for perceived versus counted patterns.
- Connect to stereotypes, health claims, or study advice.

## Teacher Notes

This activity works well before correlation, causation, or statistics units.

Make students count all cells so the hidden comparison becomes visible.
