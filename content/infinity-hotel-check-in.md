## Purpose

Students distinguish intuitive plausibility from formal mathematical coherence.

Use a physical room-shifting model to make countable infinity discussable.

## Materials

- Numbered room cards.
- Guest cards.
- Move rule card showing $n → n + 1$ and claim card $∞ + 1 = ∞$.

## Setup Notes

- Use numbered cards on desks or around the room to represent hotel rooms.
- Make clear when students are reasoning about a formal model rather than a physically possible hotel.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- One new guest arrives at a full infinite hotel; every current guest moves by $n → n + 1$.
- Claim card: $∞ + 1 = ∞$ feels impossible until the rule is specified.

## Run Resources

- Room card set and move-rule card.
- Reflection sheet: what felt impossible, what the rule showed, and what the model assumes.

## Student Prompt

Inspect the Infinity Hotel Check-In stimulus. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Actual Lesson Questions

1. What is your first knowledge claim after inspecting the Infinity Hotel Check-In stimulus?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. What specific evidence did you use?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. Can mathematics know beyond intuition?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Concrete Stimulus Packet

### Pattern or Proof Card

Sequence A: 2, 4, 8, 16, 32. Student claim: “The next number must be 64.” Alternative rule: double until 32, then subtract 10. Both fit the visible pattern so far.

Teacher cue: Use this to separate pattern confidence from proof or model choice.

### Infinity Hotel Check-In Example

One new guest arrives at a full infinite hotel; every current guest moves by $n → n + 1$.

Teacher cue: Use this as the activity-specific version of the stimulus.

### Comparison / Twist Card

Claim card: $∞ + 1 = ∞$ feels impossible until the rule is specified.

Teacher cue: Reveal after students commit to their first judgement.


## Student Task

Use the stimulus cards to complete the observation/inference/evidence/confidence grid, then answer one TOK knowledge question connected to Mathematics.

## Teacher Key / Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how mathematical concepts can be coherent even when they violate ordinary experience.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Procedure

1. Assign students numbered hotel rooms and announce that every room is occupied.
2. Introduce one new guest and ask whether the hotel can make room.
3. Use the rule $n → n + 1$ so each guest moves to the next room.
4. Discuss why the model works for countably infinite rooms but not for a finite hotel.
5. Debrief the difference between everyday imagination and formal definition.

## Teacher Language

- Do not ask whether a real hotel could do this; ask whether the formal rule is coherent.
- Where did your everyday intuition stop helping?

## TOK Debrief

- Knowledge question: Can mathematics produce knowledge about things we cannot imagine concretely?
- Why can a coherent formal system feel impossible?
- What is the role of intuition in mathematical knowledge?

## Knowledge Questions

- Can mathematics know beyond intuition?
- What makes a mathematical object meaningful?
- When should intuition be trusted in mathematics?

## Exhibition Object Idea

A numbered room card sequence illustrating the move rule $n → n + 1$.

## Sample Student Output

- The model was coherent even though I could not picture the whole hotel.
- I learned that mathematical possibility can depend on definitions rather than physical imagination.

## Adaptations

- Support: act out the first ten rooms only.
- Challenge: add infinitely many new guests by moving each current guest from room n to room 2n.

## Extension Tasks

- Compare countable infinity with a finite hotel and identify exactly where the rule fails.
- Ask whether mathematical objects need real-world counterparts to produce knowledge.

## Teacher Notes

Clarify that this is a model of countable infinity, not a trick about real hotels.

Let students feel the paradox before formalising the rule.
