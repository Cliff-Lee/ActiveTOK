## Purpose

Students identify absences, silences, and preservation bias in historical evidence.

Use a source packet to show that missing evidence is itself a knowledge problem.

## Materials

- Fictional or real source packet with several perspectives present and at least one missing.
- Silence audit sheet.

## Setup Notes

- Prepare a packet where the missing perspective is inferable but not recoverable.
- Make students mark uncertainty rather than inventing missing testimony.

## Ready-to-Use Examples

- Packet: official school notice, newspaper paragraph, teacher diary, but no student voices.
- Packet: merchant account, travel diary, law code, but no record from workers affected by the policy.

## Student Prompt

Inspect the Source Silence Investigation stimulus. Record one first claim, the evidence you used, your confidence, and what would make you revise.

## Actual Lesson Questions

1. What is your first knowledge claim after inspecting the Source Silence Investigation stimulus?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. What specific evidence did you use?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. How can we know responsibly when evidence is missing?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Student Task

Use the stimulus cards to complete the observation/inference/evidence/confidence grid, then answer one TOK knowledge question connected to History.

## Teacher Key / Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how historical knowledge depends not only on surviving sources, but also on recognizing what did not survive.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Procedure

1. Give groups a short source packet about one event.
2. Students list whose voices, records, or perspectives are present.
3. Groups infer which voices may be missing and why.
4. Students revise one conclusion to include uncertainty from source silence.
5. Debrief how historians responsibly reason from incomplete archives.

## Teacher Language

- Naming a silence is not the same as filling it with imagination.
- How does the missing voice change the strength of your conclusion?

## TOK Debrief

- Knowledge question: How can we know responsibly when evidence is missing?
- Is absence of evidence evidence of absence?
- How do preservation and power shape historical knowledge?

## Knowledge Questions

- How can we know responsibly when evidence is missing?
- How do archives shape historical knowledge?
- What does source silence reveal about power?

## Exhibition Object Idea

A source packet annotated with present voices and missing voices.

## Sample Student Output

- The archive lets us know the policy existed but not how students experienced it.
- We revised our claim from 'students accepted it' to 'the available sources do not show student opposition'.

## Adaptations

- Support: provide a checklist of possible missing voices.
- Challenge: ask students to distinguish accidental absence from deliberate exclusion.

## Extension Tasks

- Students add a missing-source request: what source would most improve the account?
- Connect source silence to museums, family histories, and national archives.

## Teacher Notes

This activity pairs well with any history unit using primary sources.

Encourage students to avoid inventing missing voices while still naming uncertainty.
