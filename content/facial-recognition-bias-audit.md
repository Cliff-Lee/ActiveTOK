## Purpose

Students evaluate a technological knowledge claim using accuracy, fairness, and context.

Use fictional audit data to connect evidence quality with ethical deployment.

## Materials

- Fictional audit table with false positives and false negatives across groups.
- Stakeholder role cards and deployment decision sheet.

- Use fictional data and avoid using real student faces or sensitive biometric tools.

## Actual Lesson Questions

1. What is your first knowledge claim after inspecting the Facial Recognition Bias Audit stimulus?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2. What specific evidence did you use?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3. What did you infer beyond the evidence?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4. How confident are you: 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, or 100%?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5. What missing information would most improve the knowledge claim?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6. When is a technological classification reliable enough to act on?
   - Teacher key/cue: Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

## Student Task

Use the stimulus cards to complete the observation/inference/evidence/confidence grid, then answer one TOK knowledge question connected to Knowledge and Technology.

## Teacher Key / Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how automated recognition is a knowledge claim whose reliability can vary across contexts and groups.

- Strong response: separates observation from inference.
- Strong response: names a method, source, model, language choice, context, or perspective.
- Strong response: revises confidence when new evidence or a new criterion appears.
- Weak response: gives only opinion or says “it depends” without criteria.

## Procedure

1. Introduce a fictional school security tool and its claimed accuracy.
2. Groups inspect audit data broken down by context and subgroup.
3. Students identify where the headline accuracy hides reliability differences.
4. Role groups decide whether to deploy, revise, restrict, or reject the tool.
5. Debrief how technical evidence and ethical responsibility interact.

## Teacher Language

- Overall accuracy is not the whole knowledge claim.
- Who bears the cost when a technological knower is wrong?

## TOK Debrief

- Knowledge question: When is a technological classification reliable enough to act on?
- Can a system be accurate overall and still unjustified in practice?
- What evidence is needed before automated knowledge claims affect people?

## Knowledge Questions

- When is a technological classification reliable enough to act on?
- How should knowers weigh overall accuracy against uneven error?
- Who is accountable for knowledge produced by automated systems?

## Exhibition Object Idea

A fictional bias audit chart showing uneven error rates across contexts.

## Sample Student Output

- The system looked reliable until the errors were separated by context.
- We restricted use because the risk of false accusation outweighed convenience.

## Adaptations

- Support: use two contexts and one error type.
- Challenge: ask groups to set a minimum evidence threshold for deployment.

## Extension Tasks

- Students draft a transparency notice for any automated classification system.
- Compare algorithmic recognition with human eyewitness recognition.

## Teacher Notes

Use fictional data to keep the activity analytical and avoid identifying students.

Press students to move beyond 'technology is good/bad' toward criteria for warranted use.
