Interviewer Effect Simulation

Human Sciences 30-40 min Medium Free lesson content

Students conduct mini-interviews with different interviewer styles and compare how method shapes responses.

Free classroom activity

Big Idea

In human sciences, the person asking can become part of the evidence produced.

Teacher Summary

Students examine how interviewer behavior affects data quality.

Use roleplay to connect method, ethics, and interpretation in human sciences.

Materials

  • Interview question cards and interviewer style cards.
  • Response comparison sheet.

Actual Lesson Questions

# Question for students Teacher cue
1 Write one claim in a complete sentence. Teacher cue: look for a clear claim, not just a description.
2 List two pieces of evidence. Teacher cue: students should name visible words, data, source features, method features, or contextual clues.
3 Separate observation from interpretation. Teacher cue: this is where assumptions, framing, models, or perspective usually appear.
4 Circle one confidence level and justify it. Teacher cue: confidence is data for the debrief, not a grade.
5 Name one piece of missing information. Teacher cue: push for method, source, context, comparison, or definitions.
6 Answer using the activity as evidence. Teacher cue: students should move from the classroom example to a general TOK issue.

Comparison / Twist Card

Question: 'What makes a classroom discussion good?' Styles: encouraging, distracted, formal.

Teacher Key / Reveal

The key move is not the “right answer”; it is the moment students see how in human sciences, the person asking can become part of the evidence produced.

Setup Notes

  • Use safe topics such as homework routines, cafeteria preferences, or study environments.
  • Assign interviewer styles openly so students can analyze method rather than feel manipulated.

Ready-to-Use Examples

  • Question: 'How useful is homework for learning?' Styles: warm, rushed, leading, skeptical, neutral.
  • Question: 'What makes a classroom discussion good?' Styles: encouraging, distracted, formal.

Run Resources

  • Interview style cards.
  • Response comparison sheet: length, detail, hesitation, confidence, interviewer influence.

Procedure

  1. Pairs receive the same interview question but different interviewer style cards.
  2. Students conduct short, low-stakes interviews.
  3. Respondents reflect on how the style affected their answer.
  4. Groups compare differences in length, confidence, and content.
  5. Debrief interviewer effects and research reliability.

Teacher Language

  • The question is not the only method; the social situation is part of the method.
  • How did the interviewer change what felt safe or relevant to say?

TOK Debrief Questions

  • Knowledge question: How can data be shaped by the method used to collect it?
  • Can a neutral interview ever be fully neutral?
  • What does ethical interviewing require?

Knowledge Questions

  • How can data be shaped by the method used to collect it?
  • What makes interview evidence reliable?
  • How do ethics and method interact in human sciences?

Sample Student Output

  • The leading interviewer got shorter answers that agreed more quickly.
  • The warm interviewer produced more detail, but maybe also more desire to please.

Exhibition Object Idea

Two interview transcripts from the same question but different interviewer styles.

Adaptations

  • Support: use two interviewer styles.
  • Challenge: add inter-rater coding of response detail.

Extension Tasks

  • Students design a better interview protocol with ethics notes.
  • Compare interviews with surveys, observation, and experiments.

Teacher Notes

Keep interview topics harmless, such as study habits or preferences.

Make clear that students are analyzing method effects, not judging interviewers personally.
